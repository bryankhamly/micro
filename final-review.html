Atomic Structure

Essential Elements: CHNOPS (Carbon, Hydrogen, Nitrogen, Oxygen, Phosphorus, Sulfur)

Chemical Bonds

Covalent Bond Electrons shared between atoms; strongest bond type; found in organic compounds
Ionic Bond Electrons transferred between atoms; creates ions (cations +, anions -)
Hydrogen Bond H attracted to O or N; weak individually but strong in groups; holds DNA strands together

The Octet Rule

  • Atoms want 8 electrons in their outer shell (except H wants 2)
  • 3 or fewer valence electrons = tend to donate
  • 5 or more valence electrons = tend to receive

Protein Structure Levels

Primary: Linear sequence of amino acids (N-C-C backbone with R groups)

Tertiary: 3D folding of single polypeptide (disulfide bonds, hydrophobic interactions, salt bridges)

Quaternary: Multiple polypeptide chains combined (e.g., hemoglobin = 2Î± + 2Î² chains)

Quick Facts - Ch 5

  • Glycolysis = 10 reactions; occurs in cytoplasm; does NOT require O2
  • Krebs cycle location: prokaryotes = cytoplasm; eukaryotes = mitochondrial matrix
  • ETC location: prokaryotes = plasma membrane; eukaryotes = inner mitochondrial membrane
  • Chemiosmosis = H+ gradient drives ATP synthase
  • Feedback inhibition = end product inhibits earlier enzyme in pathway
  • O2 is the final electron acceptor in aerobic respiration (reduced to H2O)

Microbial Growth

Binary Fission

Asexual reproduction in bacteria

Parent cell enlarges

DNA replicates

Cell divides into two identical daughter cells

Growth is exponential: 2n (n = number of generations)

Generation time = time for one division (0.5-3 hours for most bacteria)

Viruses

What is a Virus?

  • Virus = "poison" in Latin
  • NOT alive - infectious particles, not organisms
  • Obligate intracellular parasites - cannot multiply without host cell
  • Described as "active" or "inactive" (not alive or dead)
  • Smallest infectious particles
  • Lack: ribosomes, mitochondria, ATP-generating metabolism
  • NOT sensitive to antibiotics; sensitive to interferon

Viral Structure

Virion Complete, infectious viral particle
Nucleic Acid DNA OR RNA (not both); single or double stranded
Capsid Protein coat; made of capsomeres; protects nucleic acid
Envelope Lipid membrane around some viruses; has spikes for attachment
Naked Virus No envelope; more resistant (harder to kill)
Enveloped Virus Has envelope; less resistant (easier to kill)

Host Range & Specificity

  • Viruses infect specific cell types based on receptor binding
  • Bacteriophage = virus that infects bacteria
  • Examples: HIV targets CD4+ T cells; Polio/Rabies target nervous system; Herpes targets skin/mucous membranes

Prions

  • Proteinaceous infectious particle - NOT a virus
  • Has NO nucleic acid - just misfolded protein (PrPSc)
  • Converts normal proteins to abnormal form
  • Causes Transmissible Spongiform Encephalopathies (TSEs)
  • Examples: Scrapie (sheep), BSE/Mad Cow (cattle), CJD/Kuru (humans)
  • Most resistant to disinfection methods

Smallpox & Growing Viruses

  • Smallpox: Eradicated! (last case 1977); 30% fatality rate
  • Vaccine uses vaccinia virus (related virus); leaves permanent scar
  • Only exists in government labs (CDC, Russia)

Growing viruses in lab: Require host cells (won't grow on agar)

  • Animal viruses: living animals, embryonated eggs, or cell culture
  • Bacteriophage: visible as clear plaques on bacterial lawn

Antimicrobial Drugs

Discovery & Key Principles

  • 1928 - Alexander Fleming: Observed Penicillium mold inhibiting S. aureus
  • Antibiotic: Substance produced by microorganism that inhibits another
  • Antimicrobial: Includes antibiotics AND synthetic drugs
  • Selective toxicity: Kills pathogen without harming host

Spectrum of Activity

Narrow Spectrum Effective on small range; targets specific component found only in certain microbes
Broad Spectrum Targets components common to most pathogens; can disrupt normal microbiota

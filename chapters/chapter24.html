Chapter 24: Diseases of the Respiratory System

Upper and Lower Respiratory Tract Infections

Respiratory System Structure

Upper Respiratory System

  • Nose: Filters, warms, humidifies air
  • Pharynx (throat): Shared pathway for air and food
  • Eustachian tubes: Connect middle ear to pharynx
  • Normal microbiota: Pathogenic microorganisms present but suppressed by normal flora

Lower Respiratory System

  • Larynx: Voice box
  • Trachea: Windpipe
  • Bronchi/Bronchioles: Branching airways
  • Alveoli: Gas exchange (Oâ‚‚ and COâ‚‚)
  • Lined with: Ciliated mucous membrane

Why Does the Lower Respiratory System Have Fewer Microbes?

The lower respiratory tract is nearly sterile due to several defense mechanisms:

  • Ciliary Escalator: Cilia beat upward, moving mucus (with trapped particles) toward throat to be swallowed or expelled
  • Mucus: Traps inhaled particles and microbes
  • Coughing/Sneezing: Expels foreign material
  • Alveolar macrophages: Phagocytic cells in alveoli
  • IgA antibodies: Present in respiratory secretions

Pertussis (Whooping Cough)

Bordetella pertussis
Type
Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-negative coccobacillus; Aerobe; Encapsulated
Signs & Symptoms
  • Starts with cold-like symptoms
  • Progresses to severe coughing fits
  • "Whooping" sound when gasping for air after coughing
  • Vomiting after coughing
  • Can last weeks to months ("100-day cough")
Pathogenesis
  1. Bacteria attach to ciliated cells in trachea
  2. Tracheal cytotoxin damages and destroys ciliated cells
  3. Cilia can no longer clear mucus (ciliary escalator fails)
  4. Mucus accumulates in airways
  5. Violent coughing is body's attempt to clear mucus
Complications
Broken ribs, brain damage in infants from severe coughing
Prevention
DTaP vaccine

Tuberculosis (TB)

Mycobacterium tuberculosis
Type
Bacteria
Characteristics
Acid-fast; Obligate aerobe; Mycolic acids in cell wall
Signs & Symptoms
  • Latent TB: No symptoms; bacteria dormant
  • Active TB: Persistent cough (3+ weeks), coughing blood, night sweats, weight loss, fever, fatigue
  • Historical name: "Consumption"
Latent TB Pathogenesis
  1. Bacteria inhaled and reach alveoli
  2. Ingested by alveolar macrophages but survive inside
  3. Multiply within macrophages
  4. Chemotactic response recruits more macrophages
  5. Forms tubercle (granuloma)
  6. Center becomes caseous (cheese-like necrosis)
  7. Bacteria go dormant (LATENT TB)
  8. Lesions calcify â†’ Ghon complexes (visible on X-ray)
Active TB Pathogenesis
  1. If immune system weakens, tubercle can rupture (liquefaction)
  2. Releases bacilli into bronchioles and bloodstream
  3. Coughing spreads aerosol (infectious)
  4. Blood vessels may rupture â†’ coughing blood (hemorrhaging)
Statistics
~30% of world infected; 1.5 million deaths/year
Diagnosis
  • Mantoux skin test (PPD)
  • Chest X-ray
  • Sputum culture
Prevention/Treatment
  • BCG vaccine (not used in US)
  • Multiple antibiotics for 6-9 months (rifampin, isoniazid, etc.)
  • MDR-TB (multi-drug resistant) is major concern

Influenza (The Flu)

Influenzavirus (Types A, B, C, D)
Type
Virus
Structure
8 separate RNA segments; Enveloped; Studded with spikes (H and N)
Key Surface Proteins
  • Hemagglutinin (H): Binds to host cell receptors; induces viral entry (H1, H2, H3...)
  • Neuraminidase (N): Helps release new viruses from cell; degrades mucus (N1, N2...)
  • These determine virus subtypes (e.g., H1N1, H3N2)
Signs & Symptoms
  • Sudden onset fever, chills
  • Severe body aches, headache
  • Fatigue, weakness
  • Cough, sore throat
  • Worse than common cold

Inhalation (droplets); Fomites (survives 24 hrs on surfaces)
Treatment
  • Tamiflu (inhibits neuraminidase)
  • Must start within 48 hours
Prevention
Annual flu vaccine (contains H1N1, H3N2, and Influenza B strains); updated yearly due to antigenic changes

Definition Small, gradual mutations in H and N genes Major, sudden changes from gene reassortment

DTaP Vaccine Coverage

The DTaP vaccine protects against three diseases:

  • D - Diphtheria: Caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae (throat membrane)
  • T - Tetanus: Caused by Clostridium tetani (lockjaw)
  • aP - acellular Pertussis: Caused by Bordetella pertussis (whooping cough)

Schedule: Given in childhood; Tdap boosters recommended every 10 years for adults

TB Skin Test (Mantoux/PPD Test)

  • Inject purified protein derivative (PPD) from M. tuberculosis under skin
  • If person has been infected (past or present), their memory T cells will recognize the proteins
  • Results in induration (raised, hardened area) within 48-72 hours
  • Positive test = exposure to TB (not necessarily active disease)
  • Follow up with chest X-ray if positive

Completed Study Outline - Chapter 24

Differentiate between the upper respiratory system and the lower respiratory system.

Upper Respiratory System: Includes the nose, pharynx (throat), and eustachian tubes. This area filters, warms, and humidifies incoming air. It contains normal microbiota, including potential pathogens that are normally suppressed.

Lower Respiratory System: Includes the larynx (voice box), trachea (windpipe), bronchi, bronchioles, and alveoli. The lower respiratory tract is lined with ciliated mucous membrane and is nearly sterile due to the ciliary escalator defense mechanism.

Why should the lower respiratory system have considerably less microbes than the upper respiratory system?

The lower respiratory system has several defense mechanisms that keep it nearly sterile:

  1. Ciliary Escalator: Cilia (hair-like structures) beat upward, continuously moving mucus with trapped particles and microbes up toward the throat where it can be swallowed or expelled
  2. Mucus: Sticky mucus traps inhaled particles and microorganisms
  3. Coughing and Sneezing: Forcefully expel foreign material
  4. Alveolar Macrophages: Phagocytic cells in the alveoli engulf and destroy microbes that reach the lungs
  5. IgA Antibodies: Present in respiratory secretions to neutralize pathogens

What are hemagglutinin and neuraminidase?

Hemagglutinin (H):

  • A glycoprotein spike on the surface of influenza virus
  • Binds to sialic acid receptors on host respiratory cells
  • Induces viral entry into the host cell
  • Named because it can agglutinate (clump) red blood cells
  • Different types designated H1, H2, H3, etc.

Neuraminidase (N):

  • An enzyme spike on the surface of influenza virus
  • Cleaves sialic acid from host cell surface
  • Helps release newly formed viruses from infected cells
  • Degrades mucus, helping virus spread through respiratory tract
  • Target for antiviral drugs like Tamiflu (oseltamivir)
  • Different types designated N1, N2, etc.

Together, H and N determine the influenza subtype (e.g., H1N1, H3N2)

Compare/contrast antigenic shift vs antigenic drift.

Antigenic Drift:

  • Small, gradual mutations in genes encoding H and N proteins
  • Occurs continuously during viral replication
  • Results in minor changes to viral surface proteins
  • Decreases host immune recognition (but some immunity may remain)
  • Causes seasonal flu epidemics
  • Reason why flu vaccine must be updated annually

Antigenic Shift:

  • Major, sudden changes in H and/or N proteins
  • Occurs when two different flu viruses infect the same cell
  • Their 8 RNA segments can swap (reassortment)
  • Results in completely new H/N combination
  • NO immunologic recognition - population has no prior immunity
  • Causes PANDEMICS (worldwide outbreaks)
  • Example: 2009 H1N1 "Swine Flu" pandemic

Key difference: Drift is slow/gradual (epidemics), while Shift is sudden/dramatic (pandemics). Pigs can serve as "mixing vessels" because they can be infected by both bird and human flu viruses.

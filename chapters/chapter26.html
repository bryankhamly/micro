Chapter 26: Diseases of the Urinary & Reproductive Systems

Sexually Transmitted Infections and Urinary Tract Infections

Urinary & Reproductive System Overview

Urinary System Structure

  • Kidneys: Filter blood, produce urine
  • Ureters: Transport urine from kidneys to bladder
    • Have valves that prevent backflow to kidneys
    • Protects against ascending UTIs
  • Bladder: Stores urine
  • Urethra: Expels urine from body

Urinary System Defenses

  • Urine is sterile (in healthy individuals)
  • Antimicrobial properties:
    • Low pH (acidic)
    • High urea concentration
  • Flushing action: Flow of urine removes pathogens
  • Urethral sphincters: Prevent entry of microbes

Normal Microbiota - Urinary System

  • Urine is sterile in bladder and above
  • Urethra: May be contaminated by skin microbiota at opening
  • Common contaminants: Staphylococcus epidermidis, other skin flora

Normal Microbiota - Female Reproductive

  • Vagina:
    • Predominant bacteria: Lactobacillus
    • Generates lactic acid
    • Keeps pH low (3.8-4.5)
    • Low pH inhibits pathogen growth
  • Candida albicans: Yeast present in 10-25% of women (normal flora)

Urinary vs. Reproductive System Diseases

Urinary System Diseases

  • Urethritis: Inflammation of urethra
  • UTI: Urinary tract infection
    • Intestinal bacteria predominate (mostly E. coli)
    • Women have 8x higher rate than men
    • Shorter urethra in females

Reproductive System Diseases (STIs)

  • >30 different microbes can cause STIs
  • ~15 million new cases per year in US
  • Treated with antibiotics (bacterial) or managed (viral)
  • Prevented by condoms and safe practices

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

  • Bacterial infection of female pelvic organs (uterus, cervix, uterine tubes, ovaries)
  • Symptoms: Lower abdominal pain, discharge, fever, chills, painful urination
  • Can lead to infertility (scarring of fallopian tubes)
  • Usually coinfection of N. gonorrhoeae AND C. trachomatis

Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)

Gonorrhea ("The Clap")

Neisseria gonorrhoeae
Type
Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-negative diplococcus
Signs & Symptoms
  • Men: Painful urination, pus discharge
  • Women: Often asymptomatic; can spread to uterus/tubes (PID)
  • Infants: Ophthalmia neonatorum (eye infection during birth) â†’ can cause blindness
Pathogenesis
  1. Attaches to mucosal cells via fimbriae (pili)
  2. Invades urethra (men) and cervix (women)
  3. Triggers inflammation
  4. Pus formation (WBCs + bacteria + debris)
Statistics
Most common reportable communicable disease in US
Treatment
  • Ceftriaxone (cephalosporin)
  • Also treat for Chlamydia (often coinfected)
  • No vaccine available
Key Facts
  • Coinfection with Chlamydia is common
  • No effective adaptive immunity (antigenic variability)
  • Antibiotics placed in newborn eyes to prevent ophthalmia neonatorum

Chlamydia (NGU)

Chlamydia trachomatis
Type
Bacteria
Characteristics
Obligate intracellular parasite
Signs & Symptoms
  • Males: Mild symptoms; painful urination; inflammation of epididymis
  • Females: Usually asymptomatic; abdominal pain when symptomatic; can lead to PID
  • 5x as many reported cases in women as men
Complications
PID, infertility, ectopic pregnancy
Treatment
  • Tetracyclines (Doxycycline)
  • Zithromax (Azithromycin)
  • No vaccine available
Key Facts
  • Most common bacterial STI in US
  • "Chlamydia is NOT a flower" - often asymptomatic but serious
  • NGU = Nongonococcal urethritis

Syphilis

Treponema pallidum
Type
Bacteria
Characteristics
Gram-negative spirochete; Slow generation time (30 hours); Relies on host for macromolecules
Transmission
Sexual contact with spirochete-containing lesions (genitals, mouth, rectum)
Stages of Syphilis

Primary Stage:

  • Small, hard-based chancre (ulcer) at infection site
  • Appears 2-10 weeks after infection
  • Painless
  • Fluid is highly infectious
  • Heals spontaneously in a few weeks

Secondary Stage:

  • Skin rashes (notably on palms and soles)
  • Lesions contain spirochetes - highly infectious
  • Lasts 2-6 weeks, heals spontaneously
  • Followed by latent period (no symptoms, not infectious)
  • Most cases do not progress beyond this

Tertiary Stage:

  • Occurs in ~25% of untreated cases
  • Develops 10-20 years after infection
  • Gummas: Rubbery masses of tissue, progressive inflammation
  • Affects skin and bones
  • NOT infectious at this stage

Neurosyphilis:

  • CNS involvement
  • Dementia, seizures
  • Rare today due to antibiotics
Congenital Syphilis
  • Transmitted across placenta to fetus
  • Most common when pregnancy occurs during latent period
  • During primary/secondary: Likely stillbirth
  • Damage to mental development
  • Preventable with antibiotics in first two trimesters
Treatment
  • Penicillin (effective at all stages!)
  • Tetracycline alternative
Prevention
  • Condoms
  • Early diagnosis
  • No vaccine available

Genital Warts / HPV

Human Papillomavirus (HPV)
Type
Virus
Virus Type
Non-enveloped DNA virus; >200 types, ~30 infect genital tract
Signs & Symptoms
  • Visible warts (HPV-6, HPV-11):
    • Most common worldwide STI
    • Mostly benign
  • Cancer (HPV-16, HPV-18):
    • Cause 70% of cervical cancer
    • Also: oral, anal, penile cancers
Cancer Mechanism
  1. Virus produces proteins E6 and E7
  2. These proteins bind to and interfere with p53 tumor suppressor
  3. p53 normally detects cell cycle mistakes and arrests growth
  4. Without p53 function = "No brakes" on cell growth
  5. Uncontrolled cell division â†’ cancer
Transmission
Skin-to-skin contact; Sexual contact
Target Cells
Squamous epithelial cells
Treatment
  • No cure - most infections resolve spontaneously
  • Topical medications for warts
  • Ablation (burning/freezing) of warts
  • Pap smears: Screening for cervical cancer (recommended within 3 years of first partner or age 21)
Prevention - Vaccines
  • Cervarix: Protects against cervical cancer (HPV 16, 18)
  • Gardasil: Protects against warts (HPV 6, 11) AND cancers (HPV 16, 18)
  • Recommended for boys AND girls age 11-12

Genital Herpes

Herpes simplex virus type 2 (HSV-2)
Type
Virus
Virus Type
Enveloped DNA virus (Herpesvirus)
Signs & Symptoms
  • Painful vesicles (blisters) on genitals
  • Painful urination
  • Flu-like symptoms with first outbreak
  • Recurrences possible (decrease over time)
Pathogenesis
  1. Virus replicates in skin/mucous membrane
  2. Forms vesicles (blisters)
  3. After primary infection, virus travels to sacral ganglia (near base of spine)
  4. Establishes lifelong latency
  5. Can reactivate periodically
Prevalence
~25% of people >30 years in US infected
Transmission
Most often when lesions present; can transmit even when asymptomatic (viral shedding)
Treatment
  • No cure - lifelong infection
  • Antivirals (Valtrex/Valacyclovir) suppress outbreaks
  • Can reduce transmission risk
Key Fact
"Diamonds are forever, like herpes" - lifelong infection due to latency in nerve cells

Key Concepts

Stages of Syphilis

Stage Timing Signs/Symptoms Infectious?

Secondary Weeks to months later Skin rash (palms/soles); lesions with spirochetes YES - highly infectious
Latent After secondary resolves No symptoms NO - not infectious
Tertiary 10-20 years later (25% of untreated) Gummas (rubbery masses); affects skin/bones NO - not infectious
Neurosyphilis With tertiary CNS involvement; dementia, seizures NO
Key Points:
  • Primary and secondary stages are NOT disabling but ARE infectious
  • Tertiary is disabling but NOT infectious
  • Penicillin effective at ALL stages
  • Al Capone died from complications of neurosyphilis

The Tuskegee Study - Ethical Lessons

"Tuskegee Study of Untreated Syphilis in the Negro Male" (1932-1972)

  • US Public Health Service secret experiment
  • Purpose: Study progression of untreated syphilis
  • 600 Black men recruited in Alabama with promise of free treatment for "bad blood"
  • 399 had syphilis, 201 control (no syphilis)
  • Informed consent NOT collected
  • Subjects never told they had syphilis
  • Never treated - even after Penicillin became available (1943)
  • Study lasted 40 years (supposed to be 6-9 months)

Aftermath:

  • 1972: Exposed by Associated Press
  • 1973: Class action lawsuit ($10M settlement, medical benefits)
  • 1997: President Clinton issued formal apology

This led to: Modern informed consent requirements, IRB (Institutional Review Board) oversight, Belmont Report ethical guidelines

What is the structure and function of the urinary system?

Structure:

  • Kidneys: Filter blood and produce urine
  • Ureters: Transport urine from kidneys to bladder; have valves that prevent backflow to kidneys, protecting against ascending UTIs
  • Bladder: Stores urine
  • Urethra: Expels urine from the body

Defenses:

  • Urine has antimicrobial properties: low pH, high urea concentration
  • Flushing action of urine removes pathogens
  • Sphincters prevent microbial entry

What are some of the normal microbiota in our urinary system?

Urinary System:

  • Urine is sterile in healthy individuals
  • Lower urethra may be contaminated by skin microbiota (e.g., Staphylococcus epidermidis)

Female Reproductive System:

  • Vagina: Predominant bacteria are Lactobacillus species, which generate lactic acid and keep the pH low (3.8-4.5), inhibiting pathogen growth
  • Candida albicans (yeast) present in 10-25% of women as normal flora

What are the stages of syphilis?

Primary Stage:

  • Initial sign: Small, hard-based chancre (ulcer) at infection site
  • Appears 2-10 weeks after infection
  • Painless
  • Fluid in chancre is highly infectious
  • Heals spontaneously in a few weeks without treatment

Secondary Stage:

  • Skin rashes appear (notably on palms and soles of feet)
  • Lesions contain spirochetes - highly infectious
  • Lasts 2-6 weeks, then heals spontaneously
  • Followed by latent period: No symptoms, not infectious
  • Most cases do not progress beyond secondary/latent stage

Tertiary Stage:

  • Occurs in ~25% of untreated cases
  • Develops 10-20 years after initial infection
  • Characterized by gummas: Rubbery masses of tissue with progressive inflammation
  • Affects skin and bones
  • NOT infectious at this stage

Neurosyphilis:

  • CNS involvement
  • Dementia, seizures
  • Rare today due to antibiotic treatment

Congenital Syphilis:

  • Transmitted across placenta to fetus
  • Most common when pregnancy occurs during latent period
  • During primary/secondary stages: Often results in stillbirth
  • Preventable with antibiotic treatment in first two trimesters

Key Points:

  • Primary and secondary stages are infectious but not disabling
  • Tertiary stage is disabling but not infectious
  • Penicillin is effective at ALL stages of syphilis

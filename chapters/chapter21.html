Chapter 21: Diseases of the Skin

Bacterial, Viral, and Fungal Infections of the Integumentary System

View Completed Lecture Slides

Skin Structure & Normal Microbiota

Skin Structure

  • Epidermis: Outer protective layer with keratinized cells
  • Dermis: Contains blood vessels, nerves, glands
  • Subcutaneous layer: Fat and connective tissue
  • Hair follicles: Passageways for microbes into deeper tissues
  • Sweat glands: Produce perspiration with antimicrobial properties
  • Sebaceous glands: Produce sebum (lipids, proteins, salts)

Skin Defenses

  • Physical barrier: Keratinized, dry surface
  • Salt: Perspiration creates hostile environment
  • Lysozyme: Enzyme that breaks down bacterial cell walls
  • Antimicrobial peptides: Natural antibiotics in sweat
  • Sebum: Contains fatty acids that inhibit microbes
  • Low pH: Acidic environment from fatty acids

Normal Skin Microbiota

  • Staphylococcus epidermidis: Most common; coagulase-negative
  • Staphylococcus aureus: Colonizes ~20% of people in nose
  • Diphtheroids: Gram-positive, aerobic bacteria
  • Propionibacterium acnes: Anaerobic; lives in hair follicles; causes acne
  • Candida: Yeast found on skin in small amounts

Note: Skin is generally inhospitable to most microbes due to dryness and salt. Moist areas (armpits, groin) harbor more microbes, contributing to body odor.

Types of Skin Lesions

  • Vesicle: Small, fluid-filled blister (e.g., chickenpox, herpes)
  • Pustule: Raised lesion filled with pus (e.g., acne, folliculitis)
  • Macule: Flat, discolored spot
  • Papule: Small, raised bump
  • Necrotizing fasciitis: Death of muscle and tissue

Infectious Diseases of the Skin

Staphylococcal Skin Infections

Staphylococcus aureus

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-positive cocci in clusters
Virulence Factors
  • Coagulase (97% of isolates)
  • Hemolysins
  • Penicillinase
  • Capsule
Signs & Symptoms
  • Folliculitis: Infection of hair follicle; appears as pimple
  • Furuncle (Boil): Deeper, more serious follicle infection
  • Carbuncle: Multiple connected furuncles
  • Redness, swelling, pus formation
Pathogenesis
  1. S. aureus enters through wounds or hair follicles
  2. Coagulase enzyme causes fibrin deposition around bacteria
  3. Fibrin coating inhibits phagocytosis and helps adhesion
  4. Bacteria multiply and produce toxins causing inflammation
  5. Pus accumulates (dead WBCs, bacteria, tissue debris)
Treatment
  • Drainage of abscess
  • Antibiotics (watch for MRSA)
  • Warm compresses
Key Facts
  • Most serious staphylococcal pathogen
  • ~20,000 deaths/year in US
  • 25-30% of population colonized in nose
  • MRSA is major concern
  • Golden-yellow colonies on agar
  • Survives high salt, pH extremes, high temps

Necrotizing Fasciitis

Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Strep)

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-positive cocci in chains; Beta-hemolytic
Virulence Factors
  • Exotoxin A (superantigen)
  • Hyaluronidase
  • Streptokinase
  • Hemolysins
Signs & Symptoms
  • Rapid tissue destruction ("flesh-eating bacteria")
  • Severe pain out of proportion to appearance
  • Redness, swelling, fever
  • Tissue necrosis and gangrene
  • Shock (life-threatening)
Pathogenesis
  1. Bacteria enter through minor breaks in skin
  2. Spread rapidly along fascial planes
  3. Exotoxin A (superantigen) triggers massive immune response
  4. Immune system damages own tissue
  5. Blood vessel damage leads to tissue death
Treatment
  • Emergency surgical debridement
  • High-dose IV antibiotics
  • Sometimes amputation required

Hot Tub Rash & Otitis Externa

Pseudomonas aeruginosa

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-negative rod; Aerobic
Habitat
Soil, water, poorly maintained pools/hot tubs
Signs & Symptoms
  • Hot Tub Rash: Itchy, bumpy rash; hot water opens follicles allowing bacteria entry
  • Otitis Externa (Swimmer's Ear): Painful ear canal infection
  • Burn infections: Opportunistic in burn patients
Treatment
  • Often self-limiting (hot tub rash)
  • Antibiotic ear drops (otitis)
  • Resistant to many antibiotics

Chickenpox (Varicella)

Varicella-zoster virus (VZV)

Virus
Virus Family
Herpesviridae (DNA virus)
Transmission
  • Respiratory droplets
  • Direct contact with vesicle fluid
  • Dried scabs NOT infectious
Signs & Symptoms
  • Fever, malaise
  • Itchy vesicular rash (starts on face/trunk)
  • Lesions in various stages (macules â†’ vesicles â†’ crusts)
  • Highly contagious until all lesions crusted
Prevention/Treatment
  • Varicella vaccine (live attenuated)
  • Symptomatic treatment
  • Antivirals for severe cases
Key Fact
After primary infection, VZV establishes latency in dorsal root ganglia near the spinal cord. Can reactivate later as Shingles.

Shingles (Herpes Zoster)

Varicella-zoster virus (VZV) reactivation

Virus
Cause
Reactivation of latent VZV from dorsal root ganglia
Transmission
Fluid from blisters can cause chickenpox in non-immune individuals
Signs & Symptoms
  • Painful, burning vesicular rash
  • Rash follows dermatome (belt-like pattern around waist)
  • Severe stinging/burning sensation
  • Postherpetic neuralgia (persistent pain after rash heals)
Pathogenesis
  1. VZV lies dormant in dorsal root ganglion as viral DNA
  2. Stress, aging, or immunosuppression triggers reactivation
  3. Virus travels down peripheral sensory nerves
  4. Causes vesicles along single dermatome
Prevention
  • Shingrix vaccine (recommended >50 years)
  • Antivirals if started early

Cold Sores (Oral Herpes)

Herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1)

Virus
Prevalence
~90% of population infected
Transmission
Oral/respiratory contact; Saliva
Latency Site
Trigeminal nerve ganglia (near face)
Triggers
Stress, sunlight, illness
Signs & Symptoms
  • Painful vesicles near lips ("cold sores" or "fever blisters")
  • Tingling/burning before outbreak
  • Lesions crust over and heal in 7-10 days
  • Recurrent outbreaks possible
Treatment
  • No cure - lifelong infection
  • Antivirals (Valtrex/Acyclovir) suppress outbreaks

Genital Herpes

Herpes simplex virus type 2 (HSV-2)

Virus
Prevalence
~25% of people >30 years in US
Transmission
Sexual contact; most infectious when lesions present
Latency Site
Sacral ganglia (near base of spine)
Course
Recurrences decrease over time
Signs & Symptoms
  • Painful vesicles on genitals
  • Painful urination
  • Flu-like symptoms with first outbreak
Treatment
  • No cure - lifelong latent infection
  • Antivirals (Valtrex) suppress outbreaks and reduce transmission
  • Condoms reduce but don't eliminate transmission

Measles (Rubeola)

Measles virus (Paramyxovirus)

Virus
Transmission
Highly contagious; Respiratory droplets
Complications
Pneumonia (main cause of death), encephalitis, death
Signs & Symptoms
  • Koplik's spots: White spots inside mouth (diagnostic)
  • High fever
  • Maculopapular rash (starts on face, spreads to trunk)
  • Cough, runny nose, red eyes
Prevention
  • MMR vaccine (99% reduction)
  • Highly effective

Ringworm (Tinea)

Dermatophytes (various species)

Fungus
Target
Keratin (skin, hair, nails)
Transmission
  • Direct contact
  • Fomites (towels, combs)
  • Pets
Types of Tinea
  • Tinea capitis: Scalp ringworm; bald patches
  • Tinea corporis: Body ringworm; ring-shaped lesions
  • Tinea cruris: Jock itch; groin area
  • Tinea pedis: Athlete's foot; between toes
Treatment
  • Topical antifungals
  • Oral antifungals for severe cases
  • Keep area dry
Key Fact
NOT caused by a worm! Named for ring-shaped appearance. Moisture favors fungal growth.

Candidiasis (Thrush/Yeast Infection)

Candida albicans

Fungus (Yeast)
Normal Flora?
Yes - opportunistic pathogen; normally kept in check
Virulence
Forms pseudohyphae (elongated cells) that resist phagocytosis
Signs & Symptoms
  • Oral thrush: Whitish, curd-like overgrowth on tongue/mouth
  • Vaginal yeast infection: Itching, discharge
  • Cutaneous: Red, itchy rash in skin folds
Risk Factors
  • Antibiotic use (kills competing bacteria)
  • Immunosuppression
  • Diabetes
  • HIV/AIDS
Treatment
  • Antifungals (nystatin, fluconazole)
  • Address underlying cause

Key Concepts

Staphylococcus vs. Streptococcus

Feature Staphylococcus Streptococcus
Arrangement Clusters (grape-like) Chains
Gram Stain Gram-positive Gram-positive
Catalase Test Positive Negative
Key Species S. aureus (coagulase +), S. epidermidis S. pyogenes (GAS), S. pneumoniae
Skin Diseases Folliculitis, furuncles, impetigo Necrotizing fasciitis, impetigo

Herpes Virus Latency

All herpes viruses establish lifelong latent infections in nerve cells:

Virus Primary Infection Latency Site Reactivation
HSV-1 Cold sores (oral) Trigeminal ganglia Cold sores
HSV-2 Genital herpes Sacral ganglia Genital herpes
VZV Chickenpox Dorsal root ganglia Shingles
Key Point: Latent viruses cannot be eliminated by the immune system or antivirals. Reactivation occurs with stress, immunosuppression, or aging.

S. aureus Virulence Factors

  • Coagulase: Causes fibrin clot formation around bacteria; inhibits phagocytosis; allows tissue adhesion (present in 97% of isolates)
  • Hemolysins: Lyse red blood cells; damage tissue
  • Penicillinase: Beta-lactamase enzyme; destroys penicillin
  • Enterotoxins: Cause food poisoning; heat-stable superantigens
  • TSST-1: Toxic shock syndrome toxin
  • Capsule: Inhibits phagocytosis
  • Protein A: Binds antibodies incorrectly; prevents opsonization

Hemolysis Patterns on Blood Agar

  • Alpha (Î±) hemolysis: Partial hemolysis; greenish discoloration (e.g., S. pneumoniae)
  • Beta (Î²) hemolysis: Complete hemolysis; clear zone around colonies (e.g., S. pyogenes, S. aureus)
  • Gamma (Î³) hemolysis: No hemolysis; no change in agar

Fungal vs. Bacterial Skin Infections

Feature Fungal (Tinea, Candida) Bacterial (Staph, Strep)
Appearance Ring-shaped, scaly, red borders Pustules, boils, spreading redness
Location Often in moist areas, feet, groin Anywhere, often around wounds
Treatment Antifungals Antibiotics
Course Chronic, slow progression Acute, can spread rapidly

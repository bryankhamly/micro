Chapter 25: Diseases of the Digestive System

Bacterial and Viral Infections of the GI Tract

Digestive System Structure & Normal Microbiota

Digestive System Components

  • Oral Cavity: Tongue, teeth, salivary glands
  • Pharynx & Esophagus: Passage to stomach
  • Stomach: Acidic environment (HCl)
  • Small Intestine: Duodenum, jejunum, ileum
  • Large Intestine: Colon, rectum, anus
  • Accessory Organs: Liver, gallbladder, pancreas

Normal Microbiota Distribution

  • Stomach & Small Intestine: Few microorganisms
    • HCl kills most bacteria
    • Rapid movement of food through
  • Large Intestine: ENORMOUS microbial population
    • >100 billion bacteria per gram of feces
    • Mostly anaerobes and facultative anaerobes
    • E. coli is most common facultative anaerobe

Diarrheal Disease Overview

Diarrhea is an acute syndrome of the intestinal tract characterized by:

  • Increased volume and fluid content of bowel movements
  • Increased frequency of bowel movements
  • Usually a symptom of gastroenteritis (inflammation of stomach and intestine lining)

Statistics:

  • 40% of worldwide infectious disease
  • 18% of deaths worldwide
  • Major killer in developing countries, especially children

Infectious Diseases of the Digestive System

Staphylococcal Food Poisoning

Staphylococcus aureus (enterotoxin)
Type
Bacteria (Toxigenic)
Mechanism
Preformed enterotoxin in food (intoxication, not infection)
Signs & Symptoms
  • Rapid onset (1-6 hours after eating)
  • Severe cramping, nausea, vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Recovery in 24 hours (self-limiting)
Pathogenesis
  • Enterotoxin is a superantigen
  • Stimulates nerves causing cramping and vomiting
  • Toxin is heat-stable (cooking doesn't destroy it!)
  • Bacteria do NOT invade tissues
Source Foods
Custards, sauces, processed meats, sandwiches, chicken salad left unrefrigerated
Transmission
Contact with food workers who carry S. aureus (nose, hands)

Shigellosis (Bacillary Dysentery)

Shigella species
Type
Bacteria (Invasive)
Gram Stain
Gram-negative; Facultative anaerobe
Species
  • S. sonnei: Most common in US (72%)
  • S. flexneri: Common in developing countries (28%)
  • S. dysenteriae: Most severe; produces Shiga toxin
  • S. boydii: Less common
Signs & Symptoms
  • Incapacitating dysentery
  • Severe abdominal cramps
  • Bloody, mucous stool
  • Primarily pediatric disease (70% in children <15)
Pathogenesis
  1. Shigella ingested (not affected by stomach acid)
  2. Invades villus cells of small intestine
  3. Triggers phagocytosis by epithelial cells
  4. Replicates inside intestinal cells
  5. Shigella is non-motile but spreads to neighboring cells (avoids immune system)
  6. Shiga toxin destroys tissue
  7. Does NOT perforate intestine or enter blood
Infectious Dose
Very low: 10-200 cells!
Transmission
Fecal-oral; Person-to-person ONLY

Typhoid Fever

Salmonella typhi
Type
Bacteria (Invasive)
Gram Stain
Gram-negative; Facultative anaerobe
Signs & Symptoms
  • High sustained fever (103-104Â°F)
  • Diarrhea
  • Abdominal pain
  • Rose spots on abdomen
  • Possible intestinal ulcers/perforation
Pathogenesis
  • Bacteria carried systemically (spreads to liver, spleen, gallbladder)
  • Small percentage become chronic carriers (bacteria persist in gallbladder)
  • Humans are EXCLUSIVE host
Statistics
  • ~400 cases/year in US (70% from travel: India, Bangladesh, Pakistan)
  • Worldwide: 21.5 million cases/year, 25,000 deaths
Treatment
  • Vaccine available
  • Antibiotics: Ampicillin, Ciprofloxacin
  • 20% mortality without treatment
Historical Note
Typhoid Mary: Mary Mallon, NYC cook, was the first identified healthy carrier in US. Infected 47 people. Refused to stop working and died in quarantine.

Salmonellosis (Salmonella Food Poisoning)

Salmonella enteritidis
Type
Bacteria (Invasive)
Gram Stain
Gram-negative; Facultative anaerobe
Signs & Symptoms
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Fever, cramps
  • Onset: 12-72 hours after eating
  • Resolves in 2-5 days
Pathogenesis
  1. Bacteria enter M cells in intestinal lining
  2. Multiply inside vesicles
  3. Inflammatory response causes diarrhea
  4. May cross into lymphatic system/bloodstream
Reservoir
Zoonotic: cattle, poultry, rodents, reptiles (turtles!)
Source Foods
  • Contaminated eggs (hen ovaries infected)
  • Poultry (~1 in 3 chickens contaminated)
  • Undercooked meat
Key Facts
  • Most common foodborne transmitted disease in US
  • ~400 deaths/year in US
  • Antibiotics not usually needed
  • Oral rehydration therapy is key

Cholera

Vibrio cholerae
Type
Bacteria (Toxigenic)
Gram Stain
Gram-negative; Comma-shaped (vibrio)
Signs & Symptoms
  • Infection often mild or asymptomatic
  • Severe disease (1 in 20):
  • "Rice-water stool" (profuse watery diarrhea)
  • Vomiting, leg cramps
  • Can lose up to 1 liter of fluid per hour!
  • If untreated: death in <48 hours
Pathogenesis
  1. Bacteria ingested with contaminated water/food
  2. Penetrate mucous barrier and rest on epithelial cells
  3. Do NOT invade tissues
  4. Produce Cholera Toxin (CT) enterotoxin
  5. CT activates adenyl cyclase â†’ increases cAMP
  6. Elevated cAMP causes electrolytes to pump out of cells
  7. Water follows â†’ profuse fluid loss
Transmission
  • Contaminated water (poor sewage)
  • Raw/undercooked shellfish (Gulf of Mexico)
Treatment
  • Oral rehydration therapy (sugar/salt solution)
  • IV fluids for severe cases
  • No vaccine available in US
Key Facts
Affects millions in Africa and Asia; up to 50% body weight loss and 50% mortality if untreated

E. coli Diarrhea

Escherichia coli (various types)
Type
Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-negative; Facultative anaerobe
Types of Pathogenic E. coli
  • Enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC):
    • Causes traveler's diarrhea (70% of cases)
    • Produces heat-labile (LT) and heat-stable (ST) toxins
    • Stimulates fluid secretion
  • Enterohemorrhagic E. coli O157:H7:
    • Most infamous and dangerous
    • Causes intestinal hemorrhaging
    • Produces Shiga-like toxin
E. coli O157:H7 Pathogenesis
  1. Cell wall receptor fuses with host cell membrane
  2. Creates direct port to inject toxins
  3. Shiga-like toxin binds ribosomes
  4. Disrupts protein translation
  5. Kills intestinal cells â†’ hemorrhaging
Reservoir
Cattle intestine
Infectious Dose
~100 cells (very low)
Sources
  • Undercooked ground beef
  • Contaminated water
  • Fresh vegetables (spinach, lettuce)
Other Facts
  • E. coli causes 50-80% of UTIs
  • Used as indicator bacteria for fecal contamination in water/food
  • Pathogenic E. coli is 2nd leading cause of death in children (immature immune systems)

Peptic Ulcer Disease (Gastritis)

Helicobacter pylori
Type
Bacteria
Characteristics
Gram-negative; Spiral/curved bacilli; Polar flagella
Signs & Symptoms
  • Most infections asymptomatic (50% of humans colonized)
  • Abdominal pain (burning)
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Erosion/ulceration of stomach lining
Pathogenesis

H. pylori is NOT an acidophile - it doesn't love acid!

  1. Colonizes stomach (contracted in early childhood)
  2. Uses flagella to pass through mucus layer
  3. Attaches to epithelial cells beneath mucus
  4. Produces urease enzyme
  5. Urease converts urea â†’ ammonia â†’ neutralizes stomach acid locally
  6. Bacteria survive in this "ammonia cloud"
  7. Loss of mucus coating predisposes to gastritis
  8. Immune response/inflammation damages mucosa
Statistics
  • ~50% of humans colonized
  • 45% of healthy adults colonized
  • Only 0-15% develop ulcers
  • Small % develop gastric cancer (6th most common cancer)
Diagnosis
  • Blood test (antibodies)
  • Urea Breath Test (labeled urea)
  • Endoscopy with biopsy
Treatment
"Triple Therapy" (14 days):
  • Amoxicillin
  • Clarithromycin
  • Prilosec (proton pump inhibitor)
Historical Note
  • Before 1982: Ulcers thought to be caused by stress/spicy food
  • Isolated in 1984 by Marshall & Warren
  • Now known: H. pylori causes >90% of duodenal ulcers and 80% of gastric ulcers

Differentiate between Toxigenic and Invasive Diarrheal Disease.

Toxigenic Diarrhea:

  • Bacteria release enterotoxins that bind to surface receptors on intestinal cells
  • Toxins disrupt physiology of epithelial cells without invasion
  • Stimulates hypersecretion of fluids and electrolytes
  • Organism does NOT invade tissues - stays on surface
  • Stool: Large volume, watery, NO blood
  • Examples: Cholera, S. aureus food poisoning, ETEC

Invasive Diarrhea:

  • Microbes physically invade the wall of the small or large intestine
  • Damages cells lining the mucosa
  • Stool: Contains BLOOD and mucus
  • Results in severe dehydration
  • Examples: Shigella, Salmonella

How are peptic ulcers diagnosed and treated?

Diagnosis Methods:

  1. Blood test: Detects antibodies (Abs) to H. pylori
  2. Urea Breath Test: Patient ingests labeled urea; H. pylori urease breaks it down; labeled COâ‚‚ detected in exhaled breath
  3. Endoscopy: Direct visualization of stomach lining with possible biopsy
  4. Stool antigen test: Detects H. pylori antigens in stool

Treatment - "Triple Therapy Regimen" (14 days):

  1. Amoxicillin: Antibiotic that kills bacteria
  2. Clarithromycin: Antibiotic that inhibits bacterial protein synthesis (translation)
  3. Prilosec (Omeprazole): Proton pump inhibitor - blocks acid production, allowing ulcer to heal

List the virulence factors AND briefly describe how each one works to contribute to the pathogenicity of S. aureus.

  1. Coagulase: Enzyme that causes fibrin to be deposited around bacterial cells. This inhibits phagocytosis by coating bacteria and allows S. aureus to adhere to tissues. Present in 97% of all human isolates.
  2. Hemolysins: Toxins that lyse (break open) red blood cells and damage host tissues.
  3. Penicillinase (Beta-lactamase): Enzyme that destroys penicillin antibiotics, conferring antibiotic resistance.
  4. Enterotoxins: Heat-stable toxins (superantigens) that cause food poisoning symptoms including cramping, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea. They stimulate nerves in the GI tract.
  5. Capsule: Polysaccharide coating that inhibits phagocytosis by immune cells.
  6. TSST-1 (Toxic Shock Syndrome Toxin): Superantigen that can cause toxic shock syndrome.
  7. Protein A: Binds antibodies by their Fc region (wrong end), preventing proper opsonization.

Additional characteristics: S. aureus is very resistant - can withstand high salt (7-10%), extreme pH, high temperatures, and remains viable after months of air drying. MRSA (Methicillin-resistant S. aureus) is a major clinical concern.

Pathogenesis Review: Peptic Ulcer Disease, Shigellosis, Salmonellosis, and Cholera

Peptic Ulcer Disease (H. pylori):

  1. H. pylori colonizes the stomach (usually contracted in early childhood)
  2. Uses polar flagella to move through the mucus layer
  3. Attaches to epithelial cells beneath the protective mucus
  4. Produces urease enzyme, which converts urea to ammonia
  5. Ammonia neutralizes stomach acid locally, creating a protective microenvironment
  6. Loss of mucus coating predisposes to gastritis
  7. Inflammatory immune response damages the gastric mucosa
  8. Results in ulcers; long-term infection associated with gastric cancer

Shigellosis:

  1. Shigella ingested (very low dose needed: 10-200 cells)
  2. Survives stomach acid
  3. Invades villus cells of small intestine via induced phagocytosis
  4. Replicates inside intestinal epithelial cells
  5. Although non-motile, spreads to neighboring cells (cell-to-cell spread)
  6. This intracellular spread allows evasion of immune system
  7. Shiga toxin destroys tissue, causing bloody, mucous diarrhea
  8. Does NOT perforate intestine or enter bloodstream

Salmonellosis:

  1. Salmonella bacteria enter through contaminated food (eggs, poultry)
  2. Survive stomach acid and reach intestine
  3. Enter M cells (specialized intestinal cells)
  4. Multiply inside vesicles within cells
  5. Trigger inflammatory response â†’ diarrhea
  6. Bacteria may cross into lymphatic system and bloodstream
  7. Can cause systemic infection in severe cases

Cholera:

  1. Vibrio cholerae ingested with contaminated water or food
  2. Bacteria penetrate mucous barrier in small intestine
  3. Attach to and rest on epithelial cells (do NOT invade)
  4. Produce cholera toxin (CT) enterotoxin
  5. CT enters epithelial cells and activates adenyl cyclase enzyme
  6. This increases intracellular cAMP levels
  7. Elevated cAMP causes electrolytes to be pumped out of cells
  8. Water follows electrolytes â†’ massive fluid loss (up to 1L/hour)
  9. Results in "rice-water stool" and severe dehydration

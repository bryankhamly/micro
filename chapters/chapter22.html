Chapter 22: Diseases of the Nervous System

Bacterial and Viral Infections Affecting the Brain, Spinal Cord, and Peripheral Nerves

View Completed Lecture Slides

Nervous System Structure & Defenses

Central Nervous System (CNS)

  • Brain: Control center for all body functions
  • Spinal cord: Pathway for signals between brain and body
  • Protected by: Skull, vertebrae, and meninges
  • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF): Cushions and nourishes CNS; fills subarachnoid space

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

  • Nerves: Branches extending from CNS to all body parts
  • Cranial nerves: 12 pairs from brain
  • Spinal nerves: 31 pairs from spinal cord
  • Ganglia: Clusters of nerve cell bodies outside CNS

The Meninges (3 Protective Layers)

  • Dura mater: Outermost; tough, fibrous layer
  • Arachnoid mater: Middle layer; web-like structure
  • Pia mater: Innermost; delicate, attached to brain surface
  • Subarachnoid space: Between arachnoid and pia; contains CSF

Purpose: Protect brain and spinal cord from injury and infection

Why CSF is Susceptible to Infection

Cerebrospinal fluid has LOW antibody levels!

  • Blood-brain barrier limits immune cell entry
  • Few antibodies present in CSF
  • Limited complement proteins
  • Once bacteria enter CSF, they can multiply rapidly
  • This makes bacterial meningitis especially dangerous

Key Definitions

  • Meningitis: Inflammation of the meninges (membranes covering brain and spinal cord)
  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain tissue itself
  • Meningoencephalitis: Inflammation of both meninges and brain

Infectious Diseases of the Nervous System

Meningitis (Hib)

Haemophilus influenzae type b

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-negative; Aerobic; Encapsulated
Normal Flora?
Yes - part of normal throat flora
Signs & Symptoms
  • Severe headache
  • Stiff neck (nuchal rigidity)
  • High fever
  • Photophobia (light sensitivity)
  • Altered mental status
  • Nausea, vomiting
Prevention
Hib vaccine (since 1988) - dramatically reduced cases
Key Facts
  • Was leading cause of bacterial meningitis in children before vaccine
  • Capsule is major virulence factor
  • NOT the same as influenza virus (flu)

Meningococcal Meningitis

Neisseria meningitidis

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-negative diplococci; Aerobic
Transmission
Respiratory droplets; Close contact
Signs & Symptoms
  • Classic meningitis signs (headache, stiff neck, fever)
  • Petechial rash (small red/purple spots)
  • Can progress to shock rapidly
  • Potentially fatal within hours
Pathogenesis
  1. Colonizes nasopharynx (40% are asymptomatic carriers)
  2. Enters bloodstream in susceptible individuals
  3. Crosses blood-brain barrier into CSF
  4. Endotoxin (LPS) causes rapid shock and death
Prevention
  • Meningococcal vaccine (recommended for teens)
  • Prophylactic antibiotics for close contacts
Key Facts
  • Outbreaks common in dormitories, military barracks
  • Endotoxin is major virulence factor
  • Can cause rapid death from septic shock

Pneumococcal Meningitis

Streptococcus pneumoniae

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-positive diplococci; Alpha-hemolytic
Prevention
Prevnar vaccine (pneumococcal conjugate vaccine)
Signs & Symptoms
  • Classic meningitis triad: headache, stiff neck, fever
  • Often follows pneumonia, ear infection, or sinusitis
  • High mortality rate if untreated
Risk Factors
  • Very young or elderly
  • Immunocompromised
  • Recent respiratory infection
Key Facts
  • Leading cause of bacterial meningitis in adults
  • Also causes pneumonia and otitis media
  • Capsule is major virulence factor

Tetanus (Lockjaw)

Clostridium tetani

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-positive; Endospore-forming; Obligate anaerobe
Source
Soil, animal feces; enters through wounds (especially puncture wounds)
Signs & Symptoms
  • Lockjaw: Jaw muscles contract (can't open mouth)
  • Opisthotonos: Severe back arching
  • Muscle spasms throughout body
  • Respiratory failure (death)
Mechanism of Tetanospasmin
  1. Spores enter deep puncture wound (anaerobic environment)
  2. Bacteria germinate and produce tetanospasmin (neurotoxin)
  3. Toxin travels up motor neurons to spinal cord
  4. Blocks release of inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA and Glycine)
  5. Without inhibition, muscles contract uncontrollably (spastic paralysis)
  6. Death from respiratory muscle spasm
Prevention
  • DTaP vaccine (Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis)
  • Booster every 10 years (Tdap)
  • Wound cleaning

Botulism

Clostridium botulinum

Bacteria
Gram Stain
Gram-positive; Endospore-forming; Obligate anaerobe
Treatment & Uses
  • Antitoxin; Supportive care (ventilator)
  • Botox: Therapeutic use for migraines, cosmetic
Signs & Symptoms
  • Flaccid paralysis (muscles cannot contract)
  • Descending paralysis (starts at head, moves down)
  • Blurred vision, drooping eyelids
  • Difficulty swallowing and speaking
  • Respiratory failure â†’ death
Mechanism of Botulinum Toxin
  1. Toxin ingested (food poisoning) or produced in wound/infant gut
  2. Botulinum toxin is an exotoxin (most potent biological toxin known)
  3. Toxin reaches neuromuscular junctions
  4. Blocks release of acetylcholine (ACh) from motor neurons
  5. Without ACh, muscles cannot contract (flaccid paralysis)
  6. Death from cardiac/respiratory failure
Types of Botulism
  • Foodborne: Eating preformed toxin in improperly preserved foods (canned goods)
  • Infant botulism: Most common in US; spores germinate in infant gut; Never give honey to infants <1 year â†’ "floppy baby"
  • Wound botulism: Spores germinate in wound; associated with IV drug use

Poliomyelitis (Polio)

Poliovirus

Virus
Virus Type
Picornavirus (RNA virus, non-enveloped)
Transmission
Fecal-oral transmission
Signs & Symptoms
  • 99% asymptomatic or mild flu-like symptoms
  • Paralytic polio (~1%): Virus reaches CNS
  • Flaccid paralysis (usually legs)
  • Muscle wasting
  • Respiratory paralysis (required iron lung)
Pathogenesis
  1. Replicates in throat and intestinal epithelium
  2. Enters bloodstream (viremia)
  3. In some cases, crosses into CNS
  4. Destroys motor nerve cells in spinal cord
  5. Results in permanent paralysis
Prevention
  • Salk vaccine (IPV, 1955): Injected, inactivated (killed) virus
  • Sabin vaccine (OPV, 1963): Oral, live attenuated virus
  • Both highly effective
  • August 2020: Africa declared wild polio-free

Rabies

Rabies virus (Lyssavirus)

Virus
Virus Type
Rhabdovirus (bullet-shaped, RNA virus)
Reservoirs
Skunks, bats, raccoons, foxes (wild animals)
Transmission
Animal bite (direct); Aerosol in bat caves (rare)
Prognosis
Nearly 100% fatal once symptoms appear
Signs & Symptoms
  • Furious phase: Agitation, hyperactivity, hydrophobia (fear of water due to painful throat spasms), excessive salivation
  • Dumb/Paralytic phase: Paralysis, coma
Pathogenesis
  1. Virus enters through bite wound (or aerosol in bat caves)
  2. Multiplies in muscle tissue at bite site
  3. Enters motor neurons and travels up to CNS
  4. Causes encephalitis (brain inflammation)
  5. Travels to salivary glands (enables transmission)
  6. Fatal once symptoms appear
Prevention/Treatment
  • PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis): Must be given BEFORE symptoms appear
  • Includes: Rabies vaccine + HRIG (Human Rabies Immune Globulin)
  • Wound cleaning is critical
  • Pre-exposure vaccine available for high-risk individuals

Key Concepts

Tetanus vs. Botulism: Opposite Effects!

Feature Tetanus Botulism
Causative Agent Clostridium tetani Clostridium botulinum
Toxin Tetanospasmin Botulinum toxin
Mechanism Blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA, Glycine) Blocks acetylcholine release
Result Spastic paralysis (muscles contract) Flaccid paralysis (muscles can't contract)
Clinical Signs Lockjaw, back arching, rigid muscles Droopy eyelids, floppy muscles, descending paralysis
Source Wound contamination Contaminated food, infant gut, wounds
Prevention DTaP vaccine + boosters Proper food canning; no honey for infants
Memory Tip:
  • Tetanus = Tight (muscles contract, can't relax)
  • Botulism = Boggy/Floppy (muscles can't contract)

Completed Study Outline - Chapter 22

Differentiate between the central nervous system and the peripheral nervous system.

Central Nervous System (CNS): Consists of the brain and spinal cord. It is protected by the skull, vertebrae, meninges (three membrane layers), and cerebrospinal fluid. The CNS serves as the control center for processing information and coordinating responses.

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Consists of all nerves that branch out from the CNS to the rest of the body. This includes 12 pairs of cranial nerves from the brain and 31 pairs of spinal nerves from the spinal cord. The PNS carries signals between the CNS and the body's organs, muscles, and sensory receptors.

What is the purpose of meninges? List the three types of meninges.

Purpose: The meninges are three protective membranes that surround and protect the brain and spinal cord from injury and infection. They also contain cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) which cushions the CNS.

Three types (outer to inner):

  1. Dura mater: Outermost layer; tough, fibrous membrane
  2. Arachnoid mater: Middle layer; web-like structure; subarachnoid space contains CSF
  3. Pia mater: Innermost layer; delicate membrane directly attached to brain/spinal cord surface

Why is the cerebral spinal fluid susceptible to bacterial infection?

Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is susceptible to bacterial infection because it has very low levels of antibodies and complement proteins. The blood-brain barrier limits the entry of immune cells and antibodies into the CSF. This means that once bacteria cross into the CSF, they can multiply rapidly with limited immune response to stop them. This is why bacterial meningitis can be so dangerous and progress so quickly.

Describe the mechanism of action of tetanospasmin.

Tetanospasmin mechanism:

  1. Clostridium tetani spores enter a deep puncture wound (anaerobic environment)
  2. Spores germinate and bacteria produce tetanospasmin (neurotoxin)
  3. Toxin travels up motor neurons to the spinal cord
  4. Tetanospasmin blocks the release of inhibitory neurotransmitters GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) and glycine
  5. Without inhibitory signals, motor neurons fire continuously
  6. Result: Spastic paralysis - muscles contract uncontrollably (lockjaw, back arching)
  7. Death occurs from respiratory muscle spasm

Describe the mechanism of action of botulinum toxin.

Botulinum toxin mechanism:

  1. Botulinum toxin is ingested in contaminated food OR produced by bacteria growing in infant gut or wounds
  2. The toxin is absorbed and travels through the bloodstream
  3. Toxin reaches neuromuscular junctions (where nerves meet muscles)
  4. Botulinum toxin blocks the release of acetylcholine (ACh) from motor nerve endings
  5. Without ACh, muscles cannot receive the signal to contract
  6. Result: Flaccid paralysis - muscles become limp and cannot contract
  7. Paralysis is descending (starts at head, moves down body)
  8. Death occurs from cardiac and respiratory failure

Note: Botulinum toxin is the most potent biological toxin known. It is used therapeutically as Botox for migraines, muscle spasms, and cosmetic purposes.

üíâ Vaccines: Introduction & History

What is a Vaccine?

A suspension of killed, inactivated, or attenuated microbes or toxoids designed to induce artificially acquired active immunity.

üìú Historical Background

The First Vaccine - Smallpox

  • Edward Jenner, 1798 - Developed the first vaccine against smallpox
  • Related to cowpox - Milkmaids exposed to cowpox never got smallpox
  • First patient: 8-year-old boy
  • Mechanism: Injection provokes primary immune response, leading to antibodies and long-term memory cells

ü¶† Smallpox: The Disease

  • Cause: Variola virus
  • Transmission: Spread by direct contact with bodily fluids or bedding
  • Host specificity: Humans are the only natural host (not carried by animals or insects)
  • Contagious period: Most contagious with onset of rash; contagious until last smallpox scab falls off
  • Last known cases:
    • United States: 1949
    • World: 1977
üí° Key Point: Smallpox has been eradicated worldwide thanks to vaccination efforts - one of medicine's greatest achievements!

üìã Types of Vaccines

1. Live Attenuated Vaccines

  • Contains live, weakened pathogens
  • Pathogens can replicate but don't cause disease
  • Stimulates strong immune response similar to natural infection
  • Usually provides long-lasting immunity
  • Examples: MMR (measles, mumps, rubella), chickenpox, yellow fever

2. Inactivated (Killed) Vaccines

  • Uses microbes that have been killed
  • Killed using formalin or phenol
  • Cannot replicate or cause infection
  • Generally requires multiple doses (boosters)
  • Safer for immunocompromised individuals
  • Examples: Polio (injectable), hepatitis A, rabies

3. Subunit Vaccines

  • Uses antigenic fragments of the pathogen
  • Contains only the parts that best stimulate immune response
  • Some can be produced by genetic modification techniques
  • Other microbes (like yeast) can be engineered to make the antigen
  • Also called recombinant vaccines
  • Examples: Hepatitis B, HPV, pertussis (whooping cough)

4. DNA (Nucleic Acid) Vaccines

  • Naked DNA (plasmid) injected into cells
  • Cells express the antigen encoded by the DNA
  • Stimulates both humoral and cellular immunity
  • Advantages:
    • Easier to manufacture (vs whole pathogens)
    • DNA is stable (easy to transport)
    • Cost-effective for research
    • Our immune system does not see plasmid as foreign
    • Cost-effective for patients (especially in underdeveloped countries)
  • Examples: Some COVID-19 vaccines, experimental vaccines

5. Toxoid Vaccines

  • Uses inactivated toxins from bacteria
  • Toxin is chemically modified to be harmless but still immunogenic
  • Stimulates immunity against the bacterial toxin
  • Examples: Tetanus, diphtheria

üéØ The Perfect Vaccine

Characteristics of an ideal vaccine:

  • 100% effective after one dose
  • Oral administration (no injections needed)
  • Eliminates need for injections
  • Effective at protecting mucous membranes
  • Life-long immunity
  • Long shelf life; no refrigeration required
  • Inexpensive to produce

üî¨ Monoclonal Antibodies

What are Monoclonal Antibodies?

Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) are identical antibodies produced by clones of a single B cell. They are highly specific for one particular antigen/epitope.

üß´ How Are They Generated?

The Hybridoma Technique

  1. ‚ù∂ Injection: A mouse is injected with a specific antigen that will induce production of antibodies against that antigen
  2. ‚ù∑ Spleen Removal: The spleen of the mouse is removed and homogenized into a cell suspension. The suspension includes B cells that produce antibodies against the injected antigen
  3. ‚ù∏ Fusion: The spleen cells are then mixed with myeloma cells (cancerous B cells) that are capable of continuous growth in culture. Some of the antibody-producing spleen cells and myeloma cells fuse to form hybrid cells. These hybrid cells are now capable of growing continuously in culture
  4. ‚ùπ Selection: The mixture of cells is placed in a selective medium that allows only hybrid cells to grow
  5. ‚ù∫ Cloning: Individual hybridomas are cloned. The hybridomas are screened for production of the desired antibody
  6. ‚ùª Production: The selected hybridomas are then cultured to produce large quantities of monoclonal antibodies. These antibodies are used for treating and diagnosing disease

üß™ Ab-producing B Cell Can Become Cancerous

  • Myeloma - Cancer of antibody-producing B cells
  • Can be isolated and grown indefinitely in culture
  • Myeloma + normal Ab-producing cell = hybridoma
  • Clones of these cells produce mass quantities of identical antibodies
  • These are called monoclonal antibodies (Mabs)

‚úÖ Why Are Monoclonal Antibodies Useful?

  • Uniform - All antibodies are identical
  • Highly specific - Target one specific epitope/antigen
  • Produced readily in large amounts - Can be mass-produced

üè• Applications of Monoclonal Antibodies

Medical Uses:

  • Diagnostics: Pregnancy tests, disease detection
  • Therapeutics: Cancer treatment, autoimmune disease treatment
  • Research: Identifying and purifying specific molecules
  • Immunoassays: ELISA tests, Western blots

üß™ ELISA - Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay

What Does ELISA Stand For?

Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay

A laboratory technique used to detect and quantify antibodies or antigens in a sample.

üéØ Two Main Types of ELISA

1. Direct ELISA - Detects Antigens

Used to detect specific ANTIGENS in a patient's sample

‚úÖ Positive Reaction Steps:
  1. ‚ù∂ Antibody is attached to well - Start with a specific antibody bound to the plate
  2. ‚ù∑ Patient sample is added - Add the patient sample; complementary antigen binds to antibody
  3. ‚ù∏ Enzyme-linked antibody specific for test antigen is added and binds to antigen - A second antibody with an enzyme attached binds to a different epitope on the antigen
  4. ‚ùπ Enzyme's substrate (‚ñ†) is added, and reaction produces a product that causes a visible color change (‚ñ†‚ñ†) - Add substrate which the enzyme converts to a colored product

Result: Color change indicates ANTIGEN is present ‚úì

‚ùå Negative Reaction:
  • If antigen is NOT present in sample, enzyme-linked antibody won't bind
  • No color change occurs
  • Result: No antigen detected

2. Indirect ELISA - Detects Antibodies

Used to detect specific ANTIBODIES in a patient's serum

‚úÖ Positive Reaction Steps:
  1. ‚ù∂ Antigen is absorbed to well - Start with a specific antigen bound to the plate
  2. ‚ù∑ Patient serum is added; complementary antibody binds to antigen - If patient has antibodies against the antigen, they will bind
  3. ‚ù∏ Enzyme-linked anti-HIGG (anti-human IgG) is added and binds to bound antibody - A secondary antibody (with enzyme) that recognizes human antibodies is added
  4. ‚ùπ Enzyme's substrate (‚ñ†) is added, and reaction produces a product that causes a visible color change (‚ñ†‚ñ†) - Substrate is added and converted to colored product

Result: Color change indicates ANTIBODY is present ‚úì

‚ùå Negative Reaction:
  • If patient doesn't have antibodies against the antigen, nothing binds in step 2
  • Enzyme-linked antibody has nothing to bind to
  • No color change occurs
  • Result: No antibody detected

Feature Direct ELISA Indirect ELISA
Detects Antigen in sample Antibody in sample
Starts with Bound antibody Bound antigen

ü§∞ Example Application: Pregnancy Test

  • Uses ELISA technology
  • Detects hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) hormone
  • hCG is produced during pregnancy
  • Antibody specific to hCG is used in the test
  • Color change indicates presence of hCG = positive pregnancy test

Indirect ELISA

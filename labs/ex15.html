Exercise 15: Endospore Staining

Schaeffer-Fulton and Dorner Methods

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Prepare an endospore stain of bacterial cells and observe endospores in the stained preparation
  • Differentiate between vegetative cells and endospores
  • Understand the importance of endospores in disease and sterilization

Why It Matters

  • Many medically relevant pathogens produce endospores
  • Endospores are difficult to control with regular disinfection methods
  • Bacillus anthracis - causes anthrax (2001 bioterror attack)
  • Clostridium tetani - causes tetanus
  • Clostridium botulinum - causes botulism
  • Clostridioides difficile - causes diarrhea and colitis (15-30% recurrence due to endospore persistence)

What are Endospores?

When species of bacteria belonging to the genera Bacillus and Clostridium face nutrient depletion, they undergo a complex developmental cycle that produces dormant structures called endospores.

  • Endospores survive environmental conditions that are not favorable for normal bacterial growth
  • If nutrients become available again, the endospore can germinate to form a new vegetative cell
  • Endospores are very dehydrated structures that are not actively metabolizing
  • They are resistant to heat, radiation, acids, and many chemicals/disinfectants
Clinical Importance: In some cases, it is the inhalation, injection, or ingestion of endospores rather than active bacterial cells that leads to disease. For example, powdered B. anthracis spores were mailed to government offices in 2001 as part of a bioterror attack, affecting 22 people.

Endospore Structure and Resistance

Why Are Endospores So Resistant?

The chemical resistance of endospores is due to several factors:

  • Protein coat: Forms a protective barrier around the spore
  • Low water content: During sporulation, water content is reduced to 10-30% of the vegetative cell
  • Calcium dipicolinate: Spore-specific proteins form a gel that reduces protoplasmic volume
  • Thick cortex: Forms around the endospore, and contraction of the cortex results in a smaller, dehydrated structure
Key Point: The higher the water content of an endospore, the less heat resistant it will be. Calcium dipicolinate is NOT present in vegetative cells - it's unique to endospores.

Staining Challenge

The resistant properties of endospores mean that they are not easily penetrated by stains:

  • If endospore-containing cells are stained with basic stains like crystal violet, the spores appear as clear, unstained areas in the vegetative cell
  • Heat must be applied during staining with malachite green to facilitate penetration
  • Once stained, the malachite green is not removed from the endospore by subsequent washing

The Sporulation Cycle

Stages of Endospore Formation

When a vegetative cell begins to be depleted of nutrients, it enters the sporulation cycle:

  1. Nutrient depletion: Vegetative cell begins to be depleted of nutrients
  2. Chromosome duplication: The chromosome is duplicated and separated
  3. Septation: The cell is septated into a sporangium and forespore
  4. Engulfment: The sporangium engulfs the forespore for further development
  5. Layer synthesis: The sporangium begins to actively synthesize endospore layers around the forespore
  6. Cortex and coat deposition: The cortex and outer coat layers are deposited
  7. Mature endospore: The endospore reaches maturity with all protective layers
  8. Release: The free endospore is released with the loss of the sporangium
  9. Germination: If nutrients become available, the endospore swells and releases a new vegetative cell

Endospore Structure

A mature endospore has several protective layers:

Layer Location Function
Core Center Contains chromosome, ribosomes, enzymes (dehydrated)
Cortex Around core Thick peptidoglycan layer; contraction causes dehydration
Endospore coat Around cortex Protein barrier; chemical resistance
Exosporium Outermost Loose covering (not always present)

Germination

When favorable conditions return:

  • The endospore can go through the process of germination
  • The endospore swells as it takes up water
  • A new vegetative cell emerges from the spore coat
  • Growth will resume normally
Clinical significance: Recurrence of treated disease can occur due to endospore persistence. This is a common occurrence in 15-30% of C. diff patients - the endospores survive treatment and germinate after antibiotic therapy ends.

Laboratory Procedures

Schaeffer-Fulton Method (Primary Method)

This method utilizes malachite green to stain the endospore and safranin to stain the vegetative portion of the cell.

Materials:

  • 24-36 hour nutrient agar slant culture of Bacillus cereus (or other Bacillus sp.)
  • Electric hot plate and small beaker (50 ml)
  • Endospore-staining kit (5% malachite green and safranin)
  • Wash bottle
  • Bibulous paper

Procedure:

  1. Prepare smear: Prepare a smear of Bacillus cereus and allow it to air-dry
  2. Heat-fix: Heat-fix the dried smear
  3. Apply malachite green with steam:
    • Cover the smear with a small piece of bibulous paper
    • Saturate it with malachite green
    • Steam over boiling water for 7 minutes
    • The bibulous paper should be small enough that it does not hang over the edges of the slide
    • Add additional stain as needed to keep the paper saturated
  4. Cool and rinse:
    • After the slide has cooled sufficiently, remove the paper
    • Rinse with water for 30 seconds
  5. Counterstain:
    • Counterstain with safranin for about 30 seconds
  6. Rinse: Rinse briefly with water to remove excess safranin
  7. Dry and examine:
    • Blot dry with bibulous paper
    • Examine the slide under oil immersion

Expected Results (Schaeffer-Fulton)

Structure Color Explanation
Endospores GREEN Malachite green penetrates with heat; not removed by water
Vegetative cells RED/PINK Malachite green washed out; safranin counterstain taken up
Note: A variation of the Schaeffer-Fulton method that does not require steaming may be used. In this variation, a heat-fixed smear is immediately flooded with malachite green and allowed to stand for at least 10 minutes. Results will be green spores and red vegetative cells, but may be lighter than with the original method that uses steam.

Alternative Method: Dorner Method

The Dorner method is an older technique that produces a red spore within a colorless cell against a dark background.

Materials:

  • Electric hot plate and small beaker (25 ml)
  • Small test tube (10 Ã— 75 mm size)
  • Test-tube holder
  • 24-36 hour nutrient agar slant culture of Bacillus cereus
  • Carbolfuchsin
  • Nigrosin

Procedure:

  1. Make suspension: Make a heavy suspension of bacteria by dispersing several loopfuls of bacteria in 5 drops of sterile water
  2. Add carbolfuchsin: Add 5 drops of carbolfuchsin to the bacterial suspension
  3. Heat: Heat the carbolfuchsin suspension of bacteria in a beaker of boiling water for 10 minutes
  4. Mix with nigrosin: Mix several loopfuls of bacteria in a drop of nigrosin on the slide
  5. Spread: Spread the nigrosin-bacteria mixture on the slide in the same manner as in Exercise 12 (Negative Staining)
  6. Air-dry and examine: Allow the smear to air-dry. Examine the slide under oil immersion

Expected Results (Dorner Method)

Structure Color
Endospores RED (carbolfuchsin retained)
Vegetative cells COLORLESS (decolorized by nigrosin diffusion)
Background DARK (nigrosin)

Key Points to Remember

  • Endospores = GREEN (Schaeffer-Fulton) or RED (Dorner)
  • Vegetative cells = RED/PINK (Schaeffer-Fulton) or COLORLESS (Dorner)
  • Heat is required to drive malachite green into the endospore
  • Endospores are resistant to heat, chemicals, and radiation
  • Autoclaving (121Â°C under pressure) is required to kill endospores
  • Only Bacillus and Clostridium genera produce true endospores
  • Endospore-forming pathogens include agents of anthrax, tetanus, botulism, and C. diff
  • Endospore persistence explains disease recurrence (especially C. diff)

Exercise 16: Acid-Fast Staining

Kinyoun Method for Mycobacteria Detection

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Prepare an acid-fast stain of bacterial cells
  • Differentiate between acid-fast and non-acid-fast cells in a mixed stain
  • Explain the basis for the stain and why the stain is important in clinical microbiology

Why It Matters

  • The acid-fast stain is an important diagnostic tool for identifying Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB)
  • Also used to identify Mycobacterium leprae (leprosy/Hansen's disease)
  • Some Nocardia species are also acid-fast
  • Critical for rapid presumptive diagnosis of tuberculosis
  • Mycobacteria have unique cell wall properties that require special staining

What Makes Bacteria "Acid-Fast"?

Bacteria such as Mycobacterium and some Nocardia have cell walls with high lipid content. One of the cell wall lipids is a waxy material called mycolic acid.

  • Mycolic acid is a complex lipid composed of fatty acids and fatty alcohols
  • Hydrocarbon chains can be up to 80 carbons in length
  • This waxy coating significantly affects staining properties
  • Prevents bacteria from being stained by many routine stains
  • Once stained, the dye is not removed by acid-alcohol decolorization
Key Point: The term "acid-fast" refers to the fact that once these bacteria take up the primary stain, they resist decolorization by acid-alcohol - they "hold fast" to the dye.

Mycolic Acid and Cell Wall Structure

The Mycobacterial Cell Wall

The unique cell wall of mycobacteria creates special challenges for staining:

  • Mycolic acid forms a thick, waxy layer in the cell wall
  • This layer is hydrophobic (water-repelling)
  • Prevents penetration of water-soluble dyes
  • Makes the cell wall almost impermeable to most stains
  • Regular Gram staining does NOT work reliably on mycobacteria

Acid-Fast Organisms

Bacteria that are acid-fast positive:

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis - causes tuberculosis
  • Mycobacterium leprae - causes leprosy (Hansen's disease)
  • Mycobacterium smegmatis - non-pathogenic, used in labs
  • Some Nocardia species

Appearance: Stain RED/PINK (retain primary stain)

Non-Acid-Fast Organisms

Bacteria that are non-acid-fast:

  • Staphylococcus species
  • Streptococcus species
  • Escherichia coli
  • Most other bacteria

Appearance: Stain BLUE (take up counterstain)

Clinical Significance

The acid-fast stain is routinely used in microbiology labs for:

Organism Disease Sample Type
M. tuberculosis Tuberculosis (TB) Sputum, bronchial washings
M. leprae Leprosy (Hansen's disease) Skin biopsies, nasal scrapings
M. avium-intracellulare Opportunistic infections (AIDS) Various tissues

Acid-Fast Staining Methods

To facilitate staining of acid-fast bacteria, special methods must be used to make the cells more permeable to stains. Two main methods are used.

Ziehl-Neelsen Method (Hot Staining)

Principle: Uses heat to facilitate dye penetration

  • Primary stain: Carbolfuchsin (contains phenol)
  • Cells are heated for 5 minutes during staining
  • Phenol and heat facilitate penetration of carbolfuchsin into the cell
  • Heat acts as a mordant - helps dye bind to mycolic acid
Safety concern: Phenol can vaporize when heated, giving rise to noxious fumes that are toxic to eyes and mucous membranes.

Kinyoun Method (Cold Staining)

Principle: Uses increased concentrations of reagents instead of heat

  • Primary stain: Basic fuchsin (substituted for carbolfuchsin)
  • Increased concentrations of basic fuchsin and phenol
  • No heating required - safer procedure
  • The increased concentrations allow penetration without heat
Advantage: This procedure is safer because phenol fumes are not generated during staining of the cells.

The Staining Process

Step Reagent Purpose Result
1. Primary Stain Basic fuchsin (red) Stain all cells All cells RED
2. Decolorizer Acid-alcohol Remove stain from non-acid-fast cells Acid-fast = RED; Non-acid-fast = colorless
3. Counterstain Methylene blue Stain decolorized cells Acid-fast = RED; Non-acid-fast = BLUE

Why Acid-Fast Cells Retain the Stain

In the acid-fast staining procedure:

  1. Acid-fast bacteria (like Mycobacterium) are NOT decolorized by acid-alcohol
  2. They are stained pink to red by the basic fuchsin
  3. Non-acid-fast bacteria (like Staphylococcus) ARE decolorized by acid-alcohol
  4. A secondary stain (methylene blue) must be applied to visualize them
  5. Non-acid-fast cells appear blue after staining is completed
Memory tip: Acid-fast organisms retain the RED stain and resist decolorization because of their waxy mycolic acid layer.

Laboratory Procedure (Kinyoun Method)

Materials Required

  • Slant culture of Mycobacterium smegmatis (48-hour culture)
  • Broth culture of Staphylococcus epidermidis
  • Acid-fast staining kit:
    • Basic fuchsin (primary stain)
    • Acid-alcohol (decolorizer)
    • Methylene blue (counterstain)
  • Microscope slides
  • Inoculating needle and loop
Note: M. smegmatis is a non-pathogenic, acid-fast rod that occurs in soil and on external genitalia of humans. S. epidermidis is a non-acid-fast coccus that is part of normal skin microbiota (but can cause hospital-acquired infections).

Smear Preparation

  1. Prepare a mixed culture smear by placing two loopfuls of S. epidermidis on a slide
  2. Transfer a small amount of M. smegmatis to the broth on the slide with an inoculating needle
  3. Since mycobacteria are waxy, they tend to clump together
  4. Break up the masses of organisms with the inoculating needle
  5. After air-drying the smear, heat-fix it

Kinyoun Staining Procedure

  1. Cover with basic fuchsin
    • Cover the heat-fixed smear with basic fuchsin
    • Stain for 5 minutes
    • (Note: If using Ziehl-Neelsen method, continuously add carbolfuchsin with the slide over steam)
  2. Wash
    • Wash with water and shake off any excess water
  3. Decolorize with acid-alcohol
    • Decolorize for approximately 1 minute
    • Check by tilting the slide and adding more acid-alcohol
    • Continue until no more stain runs off the smear
  4. Stop decolorization
    • Stop decolorization by rinsing briefly with water
  5. Counterstain with methylene blue
    • Counterstain for 30 seconds
  6. Rinse
    • Rinse briefly with water to remove excess methylene blue
  7. Blot dry and examine
    • Blot dry with bibulous paper
    • Examine under oil immersion (100x objective)

Expected Results

Organism Acid-Fast Status Color Morphology
Mycobacterium smegmatis Acid-fast POSITIVE RED/PINK Rods (bacilli)
Staphylococcus epidermidis Non-acid-fast BLUE Cocci in clusters

Key Points to Remember

  • Acid-fast = RED (retains primary stain, resists decolorization)
  • Non-acid-fast = BLUE (decolorized, takes up counterstain)
  • Mycolic acid in the cell wall is responsible for acid-fast property
  • Kinyoun method is safer than Ziehl-Neelsen (no heating required)
  • This stain is critical for diagnosing tuberculosis and leprosy
  • Heat-fixing is still required for smear preparation
  • Examine under oil immersion to see individual cells clearly
  • Compare your slide to Figure 16.1 in the lab manual

Exercise 13: Capsular Staining

Anthony Method for Visualizing Bacterial Capsules

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Prepare and stain a smear of an encapsulated bacterium using the Anthony capsule staining method
  • Visualize the capsule and differentiate it from the cell body
  • Understand the importance of capsules in bacterial virulence

Why It Matters

  • Capsules are protective structures for disease-causing organisms
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae capsules prevent phagocytic white blood cells from engulfing and destroying the pathogen
  • This enables the organism to invade the lungs and cause pneumonia
  • Streptococcus mutans produces a capsule that facilitates attachment to tooth surfaces, leading to dental plaque and caries
  • Capsules are important virulence factors in many pathogens

What is a Capsule?

Many bacterial cells are surrounded by an extracellular gel-like layer that occurs outside of the cell wall:

  • If the layer is distinct and gelatinous, it is referred to as a capsule
  • If the layer is diffuse and irregular, it is called a slime layer
  • The capsule or slime layer can vary in chemical composition
  • If made of polysaccharides, it is known as a glycocalyx (literally means "sugar shell")
  • However, the capsule in Bacillus anthracis is composed of a proteinaceous matrix
Key Point: Evidence supports that probably all bacterial cells have some amount of slime layer, but in most cases the amount is not enough to be readily discerned.

Capsules and Slime Layers

Capsule

  • Distinct and gelatinous layer
  • Well-defined boundary
  • Tightly attached to cell wall
  • Usually composed of polysaccharides (glycocalyx)
  • Sometimes protein-based (e.g., B. anthracis)

Slime Layer

  • Diffuse and irregular layer
  • Loose, ill-defined boundary
  • Less tightly attached
  • May be easily washed off
  • Present in most bacteria to some degree

Functions of Capsules

Function Description Example
Anti-phagocytic Prevents engulfment by white blood cells Streptococcus pneumoniae
Attachment Helps bacteria adhere to surfaces Streptococcus mutans on teeth
Protection Shields from desiccation and chemicals Various environmental bacteria
Biofilm formation Helps create bacterial communities Dental plaque, catheter biofilms

Clinical Significance

Streptococcus pneumoniae

  • Capsule is the major virulence factor
  • Prevents phagocytosis by neutrophils and macrophages
  • Allows bacteria to evade immune system
  • Causes pneumonia, meningitis, otitis media
  • Pneumococcal vaccines target capsular polysaccharides

Streptococcus mutans

  • Produces capsule from dietary sucrose
  • Capsule facilitates attachment to tooth enamel
  • Forms dental plaque (biofilm)
  • If plaque not removed, leads to dental caries (cavities)
  • This is why limiting sugar intake helps prevent cavities

The Anthony Capsule Staining Method

Why Special Staining is Needed

Staining of the bacterial capsule cannot be accomplished by ordinary staining procedures:

  • If smears are heat-fixed prior to staining, the capsule shrinks or is destroyed
  • Therefore, the capsule cannot be seen in heat-fixed stains
  • The Anthony method uses smears prepared from cultures grown in skim milk broth
  • These smears are air-dried but NOT heat-fixed

How the Anthony Method Works

  1. Crystal violet staining: The smear is stained with 1% crystal violet for 2 minutes
  2. Dual staining: Crystal violet stains not only the cells, but also binds to the milk proteins from the culture medium
  3. Background staining: This causes the background of the slide to stain purple
  4. Copper sulfate wash: The stain is washed off with 20% copper sulfate solution
  5. Decolorizing the capsule: Copper sulfate acts as a decolorizer, removing crystal violet from the capsules
  6. Counterstaining: Copper sulfate also serves as a counterstain, staining the capsules a very light blue

Expected Results

Under oil immersion, a properly stained capsule preparation will show:

Structure Color Appearance
Background Purple (crystal violet stain) Dark, uniform color
Cell body Dark purple Dense, well-defined
Capsule Clear or light blue halo Unstained zone around cell
Key observation: The capsule appears as a clear or light blue halo around the dark purple cells, contrasted against the purple background.

Important Considerations

  • DO NOT heat-fix - This will destroy or shrink the capsule
  • Air-dry only - Allow smear to dry naturally
  • Use skim milk cultures - Milk proteins help create background contrast
  • Do not wash copper sulfate into sink - Use proper waste containers
  • Dispose of slides in disinfectant - Do not hand wash

Laboratory Procedure

Materials Required

  • 36-48 hour skim milk culture of Klebsiella pneumoniae
  • 1% (wt/vol) crystal violet
  • 20% (wt/vol) aqueous copper sulfate
  • Waste containers for the copper sulfate
  • Container with disinfectant solution for used slides
  • Bibulous paper
  • Microscope with oil immersion lens
Organism: Klebsiella pneumoniae is an encapsulated Gram-negative rod that can cause pneumonia and other infections. It produces a prominent capsule that is easily visualized with this staining method.

Procedure Steps (Anthony Method)

  1. Prepare thin smear
    • Use two loopfuls of the organism to prepare a thin smear on a microscope slide
  2. Air-dry only - DO NOT HEAT-FIX
    • Allow the smear to air-dry only
    • Do not heat-fix as this will cause the capsule to shrink or be destroyed
  3. Apply crystal violet
    • Apply 1% crystal violet to the smear
    • Allow it to remain on the slide for 2 minutes
  4. Wash with copper sulfate
    • With the slide over the proper waste container, gently wash off the crystal violet with 20% copper sulfate
    • Caution: Do not wash the copper sulfate and stain directly into the sink
  5. Blot or air-dry
    • Gently blot the slide dry with bibulous paper
    • Or allow it to fully air-dry
  6. Observe under oil immersion
    • Observe with the oil immersion lens
    • Compare your stain with Figure 13.2 in the lab manual

What You Should See

A properly prepared Anthony capsule stain of Klebsiella pneumoniae will show:

  • Background: Purple (from crystal violet binding to milk proteins)
  • Cell body: Dark purple (crystal violet stained)
  • Capsule: Clear or light blue halo around cells (unstained by crystal violet, lightly counterstained by copper sulfate)

Key Points to Remember

  • NEVER heat-fix capsule stain preparations - this destroys the capsule
  • Air-dry only before staining
  • Capsules appear as clear or light blue halos around dark purple cells
  • The purple background is created by crystal violet binding to milk proteins
  • Copper sulfate acts as both decolorizer and counterstain
  • Dispose of copper sulfate in proper waste containers, NOT the sink
  • Place used slides in disinfectant container - do not hand wash
  • Capsules are important virulence factors in many pathogens

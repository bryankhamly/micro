Exercise 61: Blood Grouping

ABO and Rh Blood Typing

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Understand the basis of blood grouping in the ABO and Rh systems
  • Determine the ABO and Rh types of human blood
  • Interpret agglutination reactions for blood typing
  • Explain why blood typing is critical for safe transfusions

Why It Matters

  • Type O is the "universal donor" because cells lack A and B antigens
  • Type AB is the "universal recipient" because serum lacks anti-A and anti-B antibodies
  • Blood is always specifically typed to avoid transfusion reactions
  • Rh typing is critical to prevent hemolytic disease of the newborn
  • Incompatible transfusions can cause fatal hemolytic reactions

History of Blood Typing

The procedure for blood typing was developed by Karl Landsteiner around 1900. He discovered that human blood types can be separated into four groups based on antigens present on the surface of red blood cells:

  • Type A - Has A antigens
  • Type B - Has B antigens
  • Type AB - Has both A and B antigens
  • Type O - Has neither A nor B antigens

Later research (1900-1940) by Landsteiner and Wiener identified the Rh factor, named for the rhesus monkey used in the research.

Blood Type Frequencies in the United States

Blood Type Frequency Notes
Type O 45% Most common; universal donor
Type A 39% Second most common
Type B 12% Less common
Type AB 4% Rarest; universal recipient

The ABO Blood Group System

Blood typing relies on the agglutination of antigens and antibodies. When antibodies in serum (antisera) encounter their corresponding antigens on red blood cells, they cause the cells to clump together.

Antigens and Antibodies in Each Blood Type

Blood Type Antigens on RBCs Antibodies in Serum
Type A A antigens Anti-B antibodies
Type B B antigens Anti-A antibodies
Type AB Both A and B antigens Neither anti-A nor anti-B
Type O Neither A nor B antigens Both anti-A and anti-B

How Blood Typing Works

Blood typing is performed with antisera containing high titers of anti-A and anti-B antibodies:

  1. Add a drop of blood to separate samples of saline suspension
  2. Add anti-A serum to one sample
  3. Add anti-B serum to another sample
  4. Mix gently for 30 seconds
  5. Observe for agglutination (clumping)

Interpreting Results

Anti-A Reaction Anti-B Reaction Blood Type
Agglutination (+) No agglutination (-) Type A
No agglutination (-) Agglutination (+) Type B
Agglutination (+) Agglutination (+) Type AB
No agglutination (-) No agglutination (-) Type O

What is Agglutination?

Agglutination is the clumping of red blood cells caused by antibody-antigen reactions:

  • Positive reaction: Cells appear granular, clumped, or "grainy" - like sand
  • Negative reaction: Cells remain evenly suspended - smooth, milky appearance
Why does agglutination occur? Antibodies are Y-shaped molecules with two binding sites. Each antibody can bind to antigens on two different red blood cells, cross-linking them together into visible clumps.

Universal Donors and Recipients

Type O - Universal Donor

  • RBCs lack A and B antigens
  • Can donate to any blood type (A, B, AB, O)
  • Recipient's antibodies won't attack O cells
  • 45% of US population

Type AB - Universal Recipient

  • Serum lacks anti-A and anti-B antibodies
  • Can receive blood from any type (A, B, AB, O)
  • Won't attack any donor cells
  • Only 4% of US population
Important: In clinical practice, blood is always cross-matched specifically despite universal donor/recipient designations to ensure compatibility and detect other antibodies.

The Rh Blood Group System

Between 1900 and 1940, Landsteiner and Wiener discovered additional antigens on red blood cells. The Rh system was named for the rhesus monkey where it was first identified.

The Rh (D) Antigen

The Rh system actually consists of six antigens: C, c, D, d, E, e. Of these, the D factor is most important clinically:

  • Rh-positive (Rh+): Has D antigen on red blood cells
  • Rh-negative (Rh-): Lacks D antigen on red blood cells
Population Rh-positive Rh-negative
Caucasians 85% 15%
African-Americans 94% 6%
Asians 99% 1%

Hemolytic Disease of the Newborn (Erythroblastosis Fetalis)

Rh typing is critically important during pregnancy to prevent a serious condition called hemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN) or erythroblastosis fetalis.

How HDN Develops:

  1. First pregnancy: Rh-negative mother is pregnant with Rh-positive fetus (inherited from father)
  2. Sensitization: During birth, fetal blood cells cross into mother's circulation
  3. Antibody production: Mother's immune system recognizes D antigen as foreign, produces IgM antibodies (initially)
  4. Memory response: Mother now has memory B cells against D antigen
  5. Second pregnancy: If next fetus is also Rh-positive, mother produces IgG antibodies
  6. Placental crossing: IgG antibodies cross placenta and attack fetal red blood cells
  7. Hemolysis: Fetal RBCs are destroyed, causing jaundice, anemia, and potentially death

Prevention: RhoGAM

RhoGAM (Rh immune globulin) prevents sensitization of Rh-negative mothers:

  • Given at week 28 of pregnancy
  • Given again within 72 hours after delivery
  • RhoGAM contains anti-D antibodies
  • These antibodies destroy any fetal Rh+ cells before mother's immune system responds
  • Prevents mother from developing her own anti-D antibodies
Why does RhoGAM work? The passive antibodies in RhoGAM bind to and destroy fetal Rh+ cells before they can trigger the mother's active immune response and memory cell formation.

ABO Incompatibility

ABO incompatibility between mother and fetus can also cause hemolysis, but it is usually less severe than Rh disease:

  • Most common: Type O mother with Type A or B fetus
  • Mother has naturally occurring anti-A and anti-B (mostly IgM)
  • Some IgG anti-A/anti-B can cross placenta
  • Usually causes mild jaundice, rarely severe

A complete blood type includes both ABO and Rh designation:

True Universal Donor: O-negative blood (O-) can be given to anyone in emergencies. True Universal Recipient: AB-positive (AB+) can receive any blood type.

Laboratory Procedure

Materials Required

  • Blood typing kit containing 4 synthetic blood samples
  • Anti-A, anti-B, and anti-Rh antisera
  • Plastic trays with circular wells
  • Colored mixing sticks (different color for each well)
Note: In this exercise, you will work with synthetic blood that simulates human blood typing reactions for the major blood groups A, B, O, and the Rh factor. The same type of agglutination observed with authentic blood is seen.

Procedure Steps

  1. Add Blood Samples
    • Add one drop of a blood sample to each of three wells on the plastic tray
    • Label wells: A, B, Rh
  2. Add Antisera
    • Add one drop of anti-A antisera to the well labeled A
    • Add one drop of anti-B antisera to the well labeled B
    • Add one drop of anti-Rh antisera to the well labeled Rh
  3. Mix
    • Use a different colored mixing stick for each well
    • Gently mix the synthetic blood and antisera in each well
    • Mix for at least 30 seconds
  4. Observe for Agglutination
    • Look for granular appearance indicating agglutination has occurred
    • Smooth, milky appearance = no agglutination
  5. Record Results
    • Record your observations in Laboratory Report 61
    • Determine the blood type based on agglutination pattern
  6. Repeat for Additional Samples
    • Wash the plastic tray
    • Repeat steps 1-5 for remaining blood samples

Interpreting Your Results

Well A (Anti-A) Well B (Anti-B) Well Rh (Anti-Rh) Blood Type
+ (agglutination) - (no agglutination) + (agglutination) A+
+ (agglutination) - (no agglutination) - (no agglutination) A-
- (no agglutination) + (agglutination) + (agglutination) B+
- (no agglutination) + (agglutination) - (no agglutination) B-
+ (agglutination) + (agglutination) + (agglutination) AB+
+ (agglutination) + (agglutination) - (no agglutination) AB-
- (no agglutination) - (no agglutination) + (agglutination) O+
- (no agglutination) - (no agglutination) - (no agglutination) O-

Key Points to Remember

  • Use different mixing sticks - Prevents cross-contamination between wells
  • Mix thoroughly - Ensure complete reaction between blood and antisera
  • Look for clumping - Agglutination appears grainy or sandy; no reaction is smooth
  • Type O has no agglutination with anti-A or anti-B (lacks both antigens)
  • Type AB agglutinates with both anti-A and anti-B (has both antigens)
  • Rh+ agglutinates with anti-Rh; Rh- does not
  • Rh incompatibility is a major concern in pregnancy - prevented with RhoGAM

Exercise 60: Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA)

Detecting Antibodies Using Immunoassays

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Understand the basis of enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISA)
  • Perform an indirect ELISA to detect anti-HIV antibodies in patient serum as an example of disease detection
  • Interpret ELISA results (positive vs. negative)
  • Explain the role of antigens, antibodies, and enzymes in immunoassays

Why It Matters

  • ELISA is used to diagnose many viral infections: influenza, measles, hepatitis B & C, West Nile virus, HIV
  • Also detects bacterial infections: syphilis, strep throat, salmonellosis
  • HIV remains a major global health concern - 37.9 million people living with HIV worldwide
  • Early detection improves patient outcomes and reduces transmission
  • CDC estimates >1.1 million individuals infected with HIV in the United States

Key Concepts in Immunological Testing

Term Definition
Antigen A molecule (often protein) that triggers an immune response; recognized by antibodies
Antibody Protein (immunoglobulin) produced by B cells that specifically binds to antigens
Immunoassay In vitro test using labeled antibodies to visualize antigen-antibody interactions
Direct Immunoassay Uses labeled antibodies to detect unknown antigens in a clinical specimen
Indirect Immunoassay Detects specific antibodies in a patient's serum using known antigens
Enzyme Label An enzyme attached to antibody that produces a visible color change when substrate is added

How ELISA Works

Immunological testing involves specific interactions between antigens and antibodies. ELISA is highly sensitive, detecting even minute amounts of antibodies or antigens in patient samples.

The Indirect ELISA Process (for HIV Detection)

In an indirect ELISA, we detect antibodies in the patient's serum using a known antigen:

  1. Step 1: Coat wells with antigen - HIV antigen is adsorbed (bound) to the wells of a microtiter plate
  2. Step 2: Add patient serum - If patient has anti-HIV antibodies (IgG), they will bind to the HIV antigen. Other antibodies do not bind.
  3. Step 3: Wash - Remove unbound antibodies with phosphate buffered saline (PBS)
  4. Step 4: Add secondary antibody - Anti-IgG antibody linked to an enzyme (peroxidase) is added. This binds to any patient antibodies that attached in step 2.
  5. Step 5: Wash again - Remove unbound secondary antibodies
  6. Step 6: Add substrate - A colorless substrate is added. If enzyme is present (bound secondary antibody), it catalyzes a reaction producing a visible color change (green).

Positive Result

GREEN COLOR = POSITIVE
  • Patient serum contains anti-HIV IgG antibodies
  • Antibodies bound to HIV antigen in well
  • Secondary antibody (with enzyme) bound to patient antibodies
  • Enzyme catalyzed substrate â†’ color change
  • Indicates likely infection with HIV

Negative Result

COLORLESS = NEGATIVE
  • Patient serum does NOT contain anti-HIV IgG antibodies
  • No antibodies to bind HIV antigen
  • Secondary antibody washed away (nothing to bind to)
  • No enzyme present â†’ no color reaction
  • Indicates no HIV antibodies detected

HIV Testing: The Full Picture

The ELISA used for HIV is a fourth-generation immunoassay that detects antibodies to both HIV-1 and HIV-2 strains found in different parts of the world.

Important: If an HIV ELISA is positive, follow-up confirmatory tests are required:
  • Western blot - An indirect immunofluorescence assay
  • Nucleic acid test - Detects viral RNA directly
These confirm HIV infection and rule out false positives.

Why "Enzyme-Linked"?

The secondary antibody is linked (conjugated) to an enzyme - commonly peroxidase or alkaline phosphatase.

  • The enzyme acts as an amplification system
  • One enzyme molecule can catalyze many substrate reactions
  • This produces a strong, visible signal even from small amounts of antibody
  • Much safer than radioactive isotopes used in earlier immunoassays

Types of Immunoassays

Direct Immunoassay

Purpose: Detect unknown antigens in a clinical specimen

How it works:

  • Uses labeled antibodies that recognize specific antigens
  • If the antigen is present in sample, labeled antibody binds
  • Detection by enzyme reaction, fluorescence, or radioactivity

Example uses:

  • Detecting bacterial antigens in throat swabs
  • Identifying viral antigens in respiratory samples
  • Food safety testing

Indirect Immunoassay

Purpose: Detect specific antibodies in patient's serum

How it works:

  • Known antigen is attached to a solid surface
  • Patient serum is added - antibodies bind if present
  • Secondary labeled antibody detects bound patient antibodies

Example uses:

  • HIV antibody screening (this exercise)
  • Hepatitis B and C testing
  • Autoimmune disease diagnosis
  • Allergy testing

Labeling Methods for Antibodies

Antibodies can be labeled (tagged) with different detection systems:

Label Type Detection Method Notes
Enzymes Color change when substrate added Safe, stable, widely used (ELISA)
Fluorescent dyes Glow under UV light Used in immunofluorescence microscopy
Radioactive isotopes Radiation detection Very sensitive but hazardous; less common now
Colloidal gold Visible color (pink/red line) Used in rapid lateral flow tests (pregnancy tests, COVID rapid tests)

Clinical Applications of ELISA

ELISA is one of the most widely used diagnostic techniques in clinical laboratories:

Viral Infections

  • HIV-1 and HIV-2
  • Hepatitis A, B, C
  • West Nile virus
  • Influenza
  • Measles
  • SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19)

Bacterial Infections

  • Syphilis (Treponema pallidum)
  • Lyme disease (Borrelia)
  • Strep throat
  • Salmonellosis
  • H. pylori

Laboratory Procedure

Materials Required

  • Microtiter plate (4 rows of 3 wells each)
  • Micropipetter and sterile tips
  • Phosphate buffered saline (PBS) in small beaker
  • Small waste beaker
  • Biohazard bag for used tips
  • Reagents in labeled microcentrifuge tubes:
    • HIV antigen
    • Positive (+) control
    • Patient serum #1 (PS1)
    • Patient serum #2 (PS2)
    • Secondary antibody (anti-IgG with peroxidase)
    • Substrate
Note: Gloves should be worn throughout this exercise, replicating the procedure as it would be conducted in a medical laboratory.

Key Points to Remember

  • Change tips appropriately - Prevents cross-contamination between samples
  • Washing is critical - Removes unbound antibodies to prevent false positives
  • Controls are essential - Positive control validates the test; negative control shows baseline
  • Incubation times matter - Allow sufficient time for binding reactions
  • Color interpretation - Green = positive (antibodies detected), Colorless = negative
  • This is a screening test - Positive results require confirmatory testing (Western blot or nucleic acid test)

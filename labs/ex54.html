Exercise 54: The Streptococci and Enterococci

Isolation and Identification

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Isolate streptococci from a mixed culture or throat using selective media and culturing techniques
  • Identify unknown streptococci using selective media and biochemical tests specific for streptococci
  • Differentiate between alpha, beta, and gamma hemolysis on blood agar
  • Use the Lancefield classification system to identify streptococcal groups

Why It Matters

  • Streptococcus and Enterococcus contain many medically important human pathogens
  • Common causes of pharyngitis (strep throat), sepsis, meningitis, and endocarditis
  • Hemolysis type and antibiotic sensitivity are key to species identification
  • Clinical labs use these techniques daily to diagnose infections

Streptococci vs. Staphylococci - Key Differences

Feature Streptococci/Enterococci Staphylococci
Cell Arrangement Chains (or pairs) Clusters (grape-like)
Gram Stain Gram-positive cocci Gram-positive cocci
Catalase Test Negative (no bubbles) Positive (bubbles)
Oxygen Requirements Facultative anaerobes (generally non-motile) Facultative anaerobes
Classification Lancefield groups (A, B, C, D, etc.) Coagulase test
Key Point: The catalase test is the quickest way to differentiate streptococci (catalase-negative) from staphylococci (catalase-positive). Catalase breaks down hydrogen peroxide into water and oxygen - bubbles = positive.

Types of Hemolysis on Blood Agar

When streptococci grow on blood agar plates, they produce hemolysins that affect red blood cells differently. The hemolysis pattern is a primary identification characteristic.

Beta (Î²) Hemolysis - Complete Lysis

Appearance: Clear, transparent zone around colonies - complete destruction of red blood cells

What happens: Hemolysins completely lyse RBCs, releasing hemoglobin which is then degraded, leaving a clear zone

Important Beta-Hemolytic Species:
  • Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A) - strep throat, necrotizing fasciitis
  • Streptococcus agalactiae (Group B) - neonatal meningitis
  • Streptococcus dysgalactiae (Group C) - zoonotic infections

Alpha (Î±) Hemolysis - Partial Lysis

Appearance: Greenish discoloration around colonies - partial hemolysis

What happens: Hemolysins partially break down hemoglobin to methemoglobin, producing a green/brown color

Important Alpha-Hemolytic Species:
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae - pneumonia, meningitis, otitis media
  • Viridans streptococci (S. mitis, S. salivarius, S. mutans) - dental caries, endocarditis

Note: Some species show "alpha-prime" hemolysis - a wider zone of partial lysis that can be mistaken for beta-hemolysis. Always examine stabs under magnification!

Gamma (Î³) Hemolysis - No Hemolysis

Appearance: No change in the blood agar around colonies

What happens: No hemolytic enzymes produced - RBCs remain intact

Gamma-Hemolytic Species:
  • Enterococcus faecalis (Group D) - UTIs, endocarditis
  • Enterococcus faecium (Group D) - hospital-acquired infections
  • Some viridans streptococci

Note: Enterococci can appear nonhemolytic, alpha-hemolytic, or rarely beta-hemolytic depending on conditions.

Reading Hemolysis - The Streak-Stab Technique

Since hemolysis develops best anaerobically (beneath the agar surface), always use the streak-stab technique:

  1. Roll swab over approximately 1/5 of the plate surface
  2. Use a wire loop to streak out three areas to thin the organisms
  3. Stab the loop into the agar perpendicular to the surface in the unstreaked area
  4. Make clean cuts without ragged edges
  5. Incubate at 37Â°C for 24 hours in a candle jar (reduced Oâ‚‚)
Why stab? Beta-hemolysis is most accurately read in the stab areas where oxygen is limited. Surface colonies may show less distinct hemolysis patterns.

Lancefield Classification System

Developed by Rebecca Lancefield in the 1930s, this system classifies streptococci based on antigenic differences in their cell wall carbohydrates (C-substance). Groups are designated A, B, C, D, etc.

Group A Streptococci (GAS)

Species: Streptococcus pyogenes

Hemolysis: Beta (Î²)

Key Tests:

  • Bacitracin sensitive (zone of inhibition around disk)
  • PYR positive
  • CAMP negative

Clinical Significance:

  • Strep throat (pharyngitis)
  • Scarlet fever
  • Rheumatic fever
  • Acute glomerulonephritis
  • Necrotizing fasciitis ("flesh-eating bacteria")
  • Puerperal fever (childbirth fever)
Humans are the only reservoir for S. pyogenes. Most common site: pharynx.

Group B Streptococci (GBS)

Species: Streptococcus agalactiae

Hemolysis: Beta (Î²) - narrow zone

Key Tests:

  • Bacitracin resistant
  • CAMP test positive (arrowhead hemolysis with S. aureus)
  • Hippurate hydrolysis positive

Clinical Significance:

  • Leading cause of neonatal sepsis and meningitis
  • Colonizes maternal genital tract (screen pregnant women!)
  • Also causes infections in adults with underlying conditions
CAMP Test: S. agalactiae produces CAMP factor that synergizes with S. aureus beta-hemolysin, creating an arrowhead-shaped zone of enhanced hemolysis.

Group C Streptococci

Species: Streptococcus dysgalactiae

Hemolysis: Beta (Î²)

Key Tests:

  • Bacitracin resistant, CAMP negative
  • Large colonies with large zone of beta-hemolysis

Clinical Significance:

  • Zoonotic infections (transmitted from animals to humans)
  • Pharyngitis, bacteremia, endocarditis
  • Less than 1% of bacteremias

Group D - Enterococci & Non-Enterococci

Enterococcal Species: E. faecalis, E. faecium

Non-Enterococcal: S. bovis

Hemolysis: Usually gamma (none) or alpha; rarely beta

Key Tests:

  • Bile esculin positive (blackening of slant)
  • 6.5% NaCl tolerance: Enterococci = tolerant, S. bovis = not tolerant
  • PYR positive (enterococci)

Clinical Significance:

  • UTIs (urinary tract infections)
  • Endocarditis
  • Bacteremia in hospitalized patients
  • VRE (Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococci) - major concern!
S. bovis & Colon Cancer: Isolation of S. bovis from blood is associated with GI malignancies - patients should be screened!

Special Case: Streptococcus pneumoniae

S. pneumoniae does NOT have a Lancefield antigen (no group designation).

Hemolysis Alpha (greenish zone)
Colony Appearance Smooth, mucoid (due to polysaccharide capsule)
Cell Morphology Diplococci (lancet-shaped pairs)
Key Test Optochin SENSITIVE (zone of inhibition)
Bile Solubility Positive (colonies dissolve in bile)
Clinical Significance Pneumonia, meningitis, otitis media
Optochin differentiates pneumococci from viridans streptococci:
S. pneumoniae = Optochin SENSITIVE (zone â‰¥14mm for 6mm disk)
Viridans strep = Optochin RESISTANT (no zone)

Physiological & Biochemical Tests

Bacitracin Susceptibility Test

Purpose: Presumptive identification of Group A streptococci (S. pyogenes)

Principle: S. pyogenes is uniquely susceptible to low concentrations of bacitracin

Method:

  • Place bacitracin differential disk (0.04 units) on streaked blood agar
  • Incubate 24 hours at 37Â°C

Interpretation:

  • Zone of inhibition present = Sensitive = Presumptive S. pyogenes
  • No zone = Resistant = NOT Group A (could be B, C, etc.)
Important: Use differential disks (0.04 units), NOT sensitivity disks which have higher concentrations!

SXT Sensitivity Test

Purpose: Distinguish Groups A and B from other beta-hemolytic streptococci

Principle: SXT disk contains trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole

Interpretation:

  • Groups A and B = RESISTANT to SXT (no zone)
  • Other beta-hemolytic strep (Group C, G) = often sensitive

Used together with bacitracin:

  • Bacitracin S, SXT R = Group A
  • Bacitracin R, SXT R = Group B
  • Bacitracin R, SXT S = Group C or other

CAMP Test

Purpose: Presumptive identification of Group B streptococci (S. agalactiae)

Principle: S. agalactiae produces CAMP factor, a protein that enhances the hemolysis caused by S. aureus beta-hemolysin

Method:

  • Streak S. aureus in a single line across center of blood agar
  • Streak unknown perpendicular to (but not touching) S. aureus streak
  • Incubate 24 hours at 37Â°C

Interpretation:

  • Arrowhead-shaped zone of enhanced hemolysis = POSITIVE = Group B
  • No enhanced hemolysis = Negative
Named for Christie, Atkins, and Munch-Peterson who developed the test.

Bile Esculin (BE) Test

Purpose: Identify Group D streptococci (enterococci and S. bovis)

Principle: Group D organisms can:

  1. Grow in the presence of bile (40% bile)
  2. Hydrolyze esculin to esculetin + glucose
  3. Esculetin reacts with ferric ions â†’ black color

Interpretation:

  • Blackening of more than half the slant within 24-48 hours = POSITIVE = Group D
  • No blackening or less than half = Negative
Best test for Group D identification! Both enterococci and non-enterococcal Group D are positive.

6.5% NaCl (Salt Tolerance) Test

Purpose: Differentiate enterococci from non-enterococcal Group D streptococci

Principle: Enterococci can tolerate and grow in high salt concentrations

Interpretation:

  • Growth (turbidity) in 6.5% NaCl broth = Enterococcus (E. faecalis, E. faecium)
  • No growth = Non-enterococcal Group D (S. bovis)

Timing: Check at 24, 48, and 72 hours. Color change purple â†’ yellow may also indicate growth.

Optochin Susceptibility Test

Purpose: Differentiate S. pneumoniae from other alpha-hemolytic streptococci

Principle: S. pneumoniae is uniquely susceptible to optochin (ethylhydrocupreine)

Interpretation:

  • Zone â‰¥14mm (6mm disk) or â‰¥16mm (10mm disk) = S. pneumoniae
  • Zone <14mm or no zone = Viridans streptococci (resistant)
Useful for distinguishing pneumococci from viridans streptococci - both are alpha-hemolytic!

Laboratory Procedure Overview

First Period: Making a Streak-Stab Agar Plate

Objective: Isolate hemolytic bacteria from throat or unknown mixture

  1. Obtain throat swab - Swab partner's pharynx (back of throat), avoiding cheeks and tongue. Look for white patches in tonsillar area.
  2. Roll swab on plate - Cover approximately 1/5 of the blood agar surface
  3. Streak for isolation - Use wire loop to streak out three areas, thinning organisms
  4. Stab the agar - Make perpendicular stabs in the unstreaked area for best hemolysis reading
  5. Repeat for unknown mixture - Use same technique on second plate
  6. Incubate - Place in candle jar at 37Â°C for 24 hours (NOT longer than 24 hours!)

Second Period: Analysis and Subculturing

Objective: Determine hemolysis type and select colonies for further testing

  1. Examine hemolysis in stab areas - Use dissecting microscope or 60x magnification
  2. Identify hemolysis patterns:
    • Beta (clear zone, no RBCs visible)
    • Alpha (greenish discoloration, partial clearing)
    • Gamma (no change)
  3. Select well-isolated colonies - Choose based on hemolysis type
  4. Subculture to TSB - Inoculate trypticase soy broth tubes (label ALPHA or BETA)
  5. Record observations in Laboratory Report 54
  6. Incubate tubes at 37Â°C for 24 hours

Third Period: Inoculations for Physiological Tests

Objective: Perform presumptive identification tests

For Beta-Hemolytic Isolates:

  1. Make Gram stain to confirm purity
  2. Blood agar plate - Streak each beta isolate with bacitracin and SXT disks
  3. Streak S. aureus perpendicular (for CAMP test)
  4. Incubate blood plates in candle jar at 37Â°C for 24 hours

For Alpha-Hemolytic Isolates:

  1. Make Gram stain to confirm purity
  2. Blood agar plate - Streak with optochin disk in center
  3. Bile esculin slant - Streak surface
  4. 6.5% NaCl broth - Inoculate one tube per isolate
  5. Incubate:
    • Blood agar plates: 24 hours in candle jar
    • 6.5% NaCl broths: 24, 48, and 72 hours
    • Bile esculin slants: 48 hours

Fourth Period: Evaluation of Physiological Tests

Objective: Read all tests and make presumptive identification

  1. Read CAMP test - Look for arrowhead hemolysis at junction with S. aureus streak
  2. Read bacitracin test - Any zone of inhibition = Sensitive (presumptive Group A)
  3. Read SXT test - Zone = Sensitive; No zone = Resistant
  4. Read bile esculin - More than half blackened = Positive (Group D)
  5. Read 6.5% NaCl - Turbidity or color change = Growth = Enterococcus
  6. Read optochin - Zone â‰¥14mm = S. pneumoniae; Resistant = viridans
  7. Complete Laboratory Report 54 with all identifications

Measuring Zone Diameters

For optochin and other disk tests, accurate measurement is critical:

  • 6mm disks: Zone must be at least 14mm diameter to be positive
  • 10mm disks: Zone must be at least 16mm diameter to be positive
  • Measure across the center of the disk, including the disk
  • Hold plate up to light for accurate reading

Key Points to Remember

  • Always confirm purity - Gram stain before physiological tests
  • Anaerobic hemolysis - Best read in stab areas, not surface
  • Candle jars - Provide reduced Oâ‚‚ for better hemolysis development
  • Temperature - Always 37Â°C (body temperature)
  • Time - Don't over-incubate blood agar (24 hrs max for hemolysis reading)
  • Multiple tests - Use combination of tests for presumptive identification
  • Confirmation - Commercial kits or serological tests confirm identity

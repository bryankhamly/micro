Exercise 43: Bacterial Counts of Foods

Standard Plate Count Method for Food Safety

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Understand the role that microorganisms have in food production and in food spoilage
  • Perform a standard plate count on a food sample to determine the number of bacteria in the sample
  • Interpret bacterial counts in the context of food safety
  • Explain why high bacterial counts can indicate potential problems

Why It Matters

  • CDC estimates 48 million people become sick from foodborne illnesses per year in the US
  • 128,000 are hospitalized and 3,000 die from foodborne illnesses annually
  • High bacterial counts can indicate spoilage or contamination
  • Coliform counts indicate fecal contamination and disease potential
  • Food safety testing protects public health

Key Concept: Bacteria in Food â‰  Spoiled Food

The presence of microorganisms in food does not necessarily indicate that the food is spoiled or that it has the potential to cause disease.

  • Some foods naturally have high counts due to microbial fermentation (yogurt, sauerkraut, summer sausage)
  • Postproduction treatments (pasteurization, smoking) significantly reduce bacteria numbers
  • Foods naturally carry bacteria from their environment (soil bacteria on vegetables)
  • Not all bacteria are pathogenic - many are harmless
However: Although high bacterial counts don't always mean spoilage, they can suggest the potential for rapid spoilage. High counts are important indicators of potential problems.

Microorganisms in Food Production

Beneficial Bacteria in Foods

Some foods are produced using microorganisms:

  • Yogurt - Fermented by Lactobacillus and Streptococcus
  • Sauerkraut - Fermented cabbage
  • Summer sausage - Fermented meat product
  • Cheese - Various bacterial and fungal cultures
  • Sourdough bread - Wild yeast and bacteria

These foods naturally have high bacterial counts due to the fermentation process, but this is expected and safe.

Natural Bacterial Associations

Many foods carry bacteria from their natural environment:

  • Green beans, potatoes, beets - Soil bacteria from harvesting
  • Grapes - Yeasts give chalky appearance
  • Other fruits - Yeasts naturally present on skin
  • Frozen foods - Bacteria remain after washing and can survive freezing

These bacteria may not be harmful or pathogenic - they're simply part of the food's natural microbiota.

Milk: A Special Case

Milk in the udders of healthy cows is sterile. However, bacteria are introduced during milking and processing:

  • Bacteria like Streptococcus and Lactobacillus are associated with the animal's skin
  • They are introduced during milking and processing
  • Pasteurization kills many bacteria introduced during processing, including pathogens
  • However, pasteurization does NOT kill all bacteria - some survive
  • Surviving bacteria can eventually cause spoilage and souring of milk

Coliform Bacteria as Indicators

One method to determine if food is contaminated with fecal material (and therefore has potential to spread disease) is to perform coliform counts.

What are Coliforms?
  • Bacteria such as E. coli that normally occur in the intestines of humans and warm-blooded animals
  • Their presence in food or water indicates fecal contamination
  • High potential for spread of serious diseases: typhoid fever, bacillary dysentery, cholera, intestinal viral diseases

Common Foodborne Pathogens

Foodborne illnesses usually result because pathogenic bacteria or their toxins are introduced into food products during processing, handling, or preparation.

Staphylococcus aureus

Transmission: Food handlers can transmit pathogens associated with the human body

Cause: Unsanitary practices - failure to wash hands before preparing or handling food

Result: Staphylococcal food poisoning from enterotoxins

Salmonella

Transmission: Intestinal bacteria from food handlers or contaminated ingredients

Associated foods: Poultry, eggs, meat products

Result: Salmonellosis - gastroenteritis with diarrhea, fever, cramps

Clostridium botulinum

Transmission: Endospores in soil contaminate vegetables; grow in improperly canned foods

Cause: Improper home-canning that doesn't kill endospores

Result: Botulism - potentially fatal paralytic disease from neurotoxin

Key point: C. botulinum endospores occur in soil. They can contaminate prepared vegetables and grow in anaerobic canned foods if not properly processed.

Campylobacter

Transmission: Associated with poultry and eggs

Cause: Undercooked poultry or cross-contamination

Result: Campylobacteriosis - most common cause of bacterial diarrhea

Escherichia coli O157:H7

E. coli O157:H7 is a particularly dangerous strain found in the intestines of cattle:

  • Fecal material from animal intestines can contaminate meat during butchering
  • The pathogen is incorporated into hamburger during grinding and processing
  • Serious illness results from eating improperly cooked hamburger
  • Cooking temperatures must be sufficient to kill the organism
Also transmitted via:
  • Fecal material from cattle contaminating fruits and vegetables (apples, lettuce, spinach)
  • Contaminated water used for irrigation
  • Cross-contamination during food preparation

Laboratory Procedure

Materials Required

Per student:

  • 3 nutrient agar plates
  • One 99 ml sterile water blank
  • Two 1.0 ml dilution pipettes
  • Sterile cell spreaders

Per class:

  • Food blender
  • Sterile blender jars (one for each type of food)
  • Sterile weighing paper
  • 180 ml sterile water blanks (one for each type of food)
  • Samples of ground meat, dried fruit, thawed frozen vegetables, or prewashed lettuce

Serial Dilution Process

To count bacteria in food, we must create serial dilutions to get countable numbers of colonies:

Step Dilution Calculation
Initial blend 1:10 20g food + 180ml water
Plate I 1:100 1ml from blend + spread on plate
Plate II 1:1,000 0.1ml from blend to 99ml water blank, then 1ml to plate
Plate III 1:10,000 0.1ml from 1:1000 dilution to plate

Procedure Steps

  1. Prepare food suspension
    • Using aseptic technique, weigh out 20 grams of food on sterile weighing paper
    • Add the food and 180 ml of sterile water to a sterile blender jar
    • Blend the mixture for 5 minutes
    • This suspension provides a 1:10 dilution
  2. Plate I (1:100 dilution)
    • With a sterile 1.0 ml pipette, dispense 0.1 ml from the blender to plate I
    • Add 1.0 ml to the 99 ml water blank
    • Use a sterile cell spreader to disperse the sample across the plate surface
  3. Prepare 1:1000 dilution
    • Shake the water blank 25 times in an arc for 7 seconds
    • Shake with your elbow on the table (as done in Exercise 19)
  4. Plate II (1:1000 dilution)
    • Using a fresh pipette, dispense 1.0 ml to plate II
    • Also dispense 0.1 ml to plate III
    • Use sterile cell spreaders to disperse samples across each plate surface
  5. Plate III (1:10,000 dilution)
    • The 0.1 ml from the 1:1000 dilution gives a 1:10,000 final dilution
  6. Incubate
    • Incubate all plates at 35-37Â°C for 24 hours
  7. Count and Calculate
    • Count colonies on the best plate (30-300 colonies ideal)
    • Record results in Laboratory Report 43
    • Calculate CFU/gram using dilution factor

Calculating Bacterial Count

To determine the number of bacteria per gram of food:

Formula: CFU/gram = (Number of colonies) Ã— (Dilution factor)

Example:

  • If you count 150 colonies on Plate II (1:1000 dilution)
  • CFU/gram = 150 Ã— 1000 = 150,000 CFU/gram

Countable Range: Plates with 30-300 colonies are considered ideal for counting. Fewer colonies may not be representative; more colonies are difficult to count accurately.

Key Points to Remember

  • Aseptic technique is critical to prevent contamination
  • Serial dilutions are necessary to get countable colony numbers
  • 30-300 colonies is the ideal countable range
  • High bacterial counts may indicate spoilage potential but don't prove it
  • Coliform counts specifically indicate fecal contamination
  • This procedure tests for total bacterial numbers, not specific species
  • Selective media would be needed to isolate specific pathogens

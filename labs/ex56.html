Exercise 56: A Synthetic Epidemic

Epidemiology and Disease Transmission Simulation

Learning Outcomes

After This Exercise You Should Be Able To:

  • Define terminology related to the field of epidemiology
  • Differentiate between common source and propagated (host-to-host) epidemics
  • Perform a simple test using detectable reagents to demonstrate how an infectious agent can be passed from person to person
  • Describe how epidemiology is used to trace the source and spread of communicable diseases
  • Explain how herd immunity can reduce the spread of communicable diseases through a population and protect susceptible individuals from infection

Why It Matters

  • Epidemiologists are "medical detectives" who trace sources of infection to break transmission cycles
  • The microbiology laboratory plays a crucial role in preventing outbreaks from becoming epidemics
  • Lab results help identify the index case - the first incident in an outbreak
  • Understanding disease spread informs public health policy (vaccination, quarantine, hygiene)
  • Real-world example: COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated these principles globally

What is Epidemiology?

Epidemiology is the study of how, when, where, what, and who are involved in the spread and distribution of diseases in human populations.

The Role of the Epidemiologist:

  • Search out sources of infection so the transmission cycle can be broken
  • Compare number of new cases with previous records to determine if an epidemic exists
  • Identify the index case (first case) in an outbreak
  • Work backward from positive cases to trace contacts and infections
  • Report suspicious or identified infectious diseases to the CDC and WHO

Historical Impact of Epidemics

Human history has been profoundly shaped by epidemics:

  • The Black Death (1340s): Bubonic plague caused by Yersinia pestis killed one-third of the European population
  • 1918 Flu Pandemic: Influenza affected one-third of the world population; rapid spread attributed to WWI troop movements and living conditions
  • Smallpox in Mexico: Brought by European explorers; thought to be a major factor in the defeat of native populations
  • HIV/AIDS: Over 36.9 million people living with HIV worldwide; has slowed economic growth in sub-Saharan Africa
  • COVID-19 (2019-present): SARS-CoV-2 emerged in Wuhan, China; reached pandemic status within months; over 5 million deaths in US alone within 7 months

Essential Epidemiology Terms

Disease Classification Terms

  • Infectious Disease: A disease caused by microorganisms that enter the body and multiply in tissues at the expense of the host
  • Communicable Disease: An infectious disease that is transmissible from one person to another
  • Index Case: The first identified case in an outbreak; helps epidemiologists trace the source
  • Carrier: An individual who harbors and transmits an infectious agent but shows no symptoms (can be asymptomatic)
  • Vector: A living carrier (usually an insect or rodent) that transmits an infectious agent to humans

Disease Frequency Terms

  • Endemic: A disease that exhibits a steady frequency over a long period in a particular region (e.g., malaria in tropical regions)
  • Epidemic: An excessive number of newly reported cases in a specific area in a given time period compared to previous records
  • Pandemic: An epidemic that spreads to one or more continents (e.g., HIV/AIDS, COVID-19)

Transmission Terminology

Term Definition Example
Direct Contact Transfer of infectious agent through physical contact between individuals Handshaking, kissing, sexual intercourse
Indirect Transmission Transfer through contaminated objects, food, water, or animals Contaminated food, doorknobs, water supply
Fomite An inanimate object that can harbor and transmit infectious agents Doorknobs, phones, keyboards, towels
Reservoir The natural habitat of a pathogen where it lives and multiplies Humans (for S. pyogenes), soil (for C. tetani)

Factors Affecting Disease Transmission

Increase Transmission

  • Environmental changes (healthcare settings, travel)
  • Crowded living arrangements
  • Extreme weather events (increase contact with vectors)
  • Changes in natural hosts or habitats for pathogens
  • Human behavior and bioterrorism threats

Decrease Transmission

  • Vaccination programs
  • Quarantine and isolation measures
  • Improved hygiene practices
  • Social distancing
  • Proper handwashing technique

Infectious Period Considerations

The length of time an individual remains infectious greatly affects epidemic spread:

  • Ebola: Highly contagious but short infectious period (2-21 days from symptoms to death) - often confined to small regions
  • HIV/AIDS: Patients are infectious for their entire lifetime - enables massive global spread
  • Chlamydia & Hepatitis B: Can be asymptomatic; carriers unknowingly transmit to others

Types of Epidemics

Epidemics fall into two main categories based on how the infectious agent is transmitted through the population. Understanding the type helps epidemiologists identify the source and implement appropriate control measures.

Common Source Epidemic

Definition: All cases arise from exposure to the same contaminated source (fomite, food, or water)

Pattern: Rapid increase in cases immediately after initial exposure, followed by rapid decline once source is identified and removed

Characteristics:

  • Many new cases occur rapidly after the initial case
  • Cases cluster around the time of exposure
  • Epidemic subsides quickly once source is identified and removed
  • Curve shows a sharp peak
Example: In late 2019, 167 people across 27 states were infected with Shiga toxin-producing E. coli O157:H7 after eating contaminated romaine lettuce from the Salinas Valley in California. Once the source was identified, recalls were issued and the epidemic quickly subsided.

Host-to-Host (Propagated) Epidemic

Definition: Infectious agent is transmitted directly from person to person through contact with the infected individual

Pattern: Gradual increase in cases over time; slower to subside as transmission continues through the population

Characteristics:

  • Cases grow more slowly over time
  • Epidemic takes longer to subside
  • New cases depend on contact with infected individuals
  • Curve shows a gradual rise and fall
Example: Influenza (flu) spreads from person to person through respiratory droplets. The 2009 H1N1 outbreak spread from person to person across continents, eventually being declared a pandemic by the WHO.

Epidemic Curve Comparison

Feature Common Source Host-to-Host (Propagated)
Source Single contaminated source (food, water, fomite) Infected individuals
Onset of Cases Rapid; many cases appear at once Gradual; cases increase over time
Curve Shape Sharp peak, rapid decline Gradual rise, slower decline
Duration Short (once source removed) Longer (continues while transmission occurs)
Control Strategy Identify and remove contaminated source Isolation, vaccination, reducing contact
Examples Foodborne outbreaks (Salmonella, E. coli) Influenza, COVID-19, measles

Determining if an Epidemic Exists

An epidemic is declared when the number of newly reported cases in a specific area during a given time period is excessive compared to previous records.

  • Epidemiologists compare current case numbers to historical baseline data
  • Notable current US epidemics include: chlamydia and pertussis (whooping cough)
  • The threshold for "epidemic" varies by disease and region

Herd (Community) Immunity

What is Herd Immunity?

Herd immunity (also called community immunity) is the principle that when a certain proportion of a population is vaccinated or already immune to an infectious agent, an outbreak is unlikely because the spread of the agent is limited.

Key Principle: Even individuals who cannot be vaccinated (infants, pregnant women, immunocompromised individuals) are somewhat protected because the spread of the infectious agent is limited in the community.

No Immunity (0% Vaccinated)

When no one in a population is immunized, a contagious disease spreads rapidly through the entire population.

  • All individuals are susceptible to infection
  • Disease spreads unchecked from person to person
  • Epidemic affects nearly everyone
  • Only natural immunity from recovery limits spread

Partial Immunity (Some Vaccinated)

When some of the population is immunized, disease spread is reduced but not eliminated.

  • Immunized individuals cannot become infected or transmit
  • Disease still spreads among susceptible individuals
  • Some unvaccinated individuals are still infected
  • Epidemic is smaller but still occurs

Herd Immunity Achieved (Most Vaccinated)

When most of the population is immunized, the spread of contagious disease is contained - even unvaccinated individuals are protected.

How It Works

  • Immunized individuals act as "barriers" to disease spread
  • Infectious agent cannot find enough susceptible hosts to spread
  • Chain of transmission is broken
  • Susceptible individuals are indirectly protected

Who Benefits

  • Infants too young for vaccination
  • Pregnant women
  • Immunocompromised individuals
  • Those with vaccine allergies
  • Elderly with weakened immune systems

Herd Immunity Thresholds

Different diseases require different percentages of immune individuals to achieve herd immunity:

Disease Basic Reproduction Number (R0) Herd Immunity Threshold
Measles 12-18 92-95%
Pertussis 12-17 92-94%
Polio 5-7 80-86%
COVID-19 (original) 2-3 60-70%
Influenza 1-2 33-50%

R0 (Basic Reproduction Number): The average number of people one infected person will infect in a completely susceptible population. Higher R0 = more contagious = higher herd immunity threshold needed.

Laboratory Procedures

This exercise uses two procedures to simulate epidemic spread and demonstrate the concept of herd immunity using safe, detectable reagents instead of actual microorganisms.

Procedure A: Simulating Disease Transmission

Purpose: Demonstrate how an infectious agent spreads from person to person and how epidemiologists trace the source (index case)

Materials

  • Numbered petri dish with unknown white powder (GloGerm or similar UV-detectable powder)
  • Only ONE student receives the "infectious" agent (visible under UV light)
  • Other students receive non-infectious powder (not visible under UV)
  • 2 Kimwipes or tissues per student
  • UV light source

Procedure

  1. Preparation: Label Kimwipes as "Round 1" and "Round 2." Wash and dry hands thoroughly. Coat your right hand with the powder, focusing on the palm surface.
  2. Round 1: On instructor's cue, shake hands with classmates using your treated right hand. Make firm palm-to-palm contact. Record each "shaker" and "shakee" on the board. After all contacts, rub hand on Round 1 Kimwipe.
  3. Round 2: Repeat handshaking with different classmates. Do NOT touch any objects between rounds. Record contacts. Rub hand on Round 2 Kimwipe.
  4. Chemical Identification: Place Kimwipes under UV light in a darkened room. Glowing = positive for the infectious agent. Record results for both rounds.
  5. Handwashing Check: Observe your right hand (which was washed) under UV light to demonstrate the importance of proper handwashing technique.

Analysis

  • Tabulate all class results on the chalkboard
  • Work backward from positive results to create a flowchart of transmission
  • Identify the index case (the person who started the epidemic)
  • This mirrors how epidemiologists trace outbreaks in the real world

Procedure B: Demonstrating Herd Immunity

Purpose: Investigate how community immunity protects susceptible individuals in a population

Materials

  • Numbered test tube with liquid (water, NaOH, or pH buffer)
  • Sterile gloves
  • Dropper for fluid exchange
  • Phenolphthalein indicator solution

The Three Test Tube Contents

Contents Represents Result with Indicator
Distilled Water Susceptible individual No color change (unless infected)
0.1 M NaOH Infected individual Turns pink with phenolphthalein
pH Buffer (pH 6.8) Vaccinated/immune individual No color change (cannot be infected)

Three Rounds with Different Immunity Levels

Round 1: 100% Susceptible Population
  • One student unknowingly has NaOH (infected)
  • All other students have water (susceptible)
  • Exchange fluid with 3 different classmates
  • Add phenolphthalein to test for infection
  • Expected result: Many students become "infected" (pink)
Round 2: 50% Susceptible Population
  • One student has NaOH (infected)
  • 50% of students have water (susceptible)
  • 50% of students have pH buffer (vaccinated/immune)
  • Exchange fluid with 3 different classmates
  • Expected result: Fewer students become infected; vaccinated students block transmission
Round 3: 10% Susceptible Population (Herd Immunity)
  • One student has NaOH (infected)
  • 10% of students have water (susceptible)
  • 90% of students have pH buffer (vaccinated/immune)
  • Exchange fluid with 3 different classmates
  • Expected result: Very few or no students become infected; herd immunity protects susceptible individuals

Key Takeaways from This Exercise

Epidemiology Principles

  • Disease can spread rapidly through direct contact
  • Tracing contacts helps identify the index case
  • Working backward from positive cases reveals transmission patterns
  • Early identification of the source helps contain outbreaks

Herd Immunity Principles

  • Higher vaccination rates = fewer infections
  • Immune individuals block transmission chains
  • Susceptible individuals are protected indirectly
  • Achieving herd immunity requires high vaccination coverage

Safety Cautions

  • NaOH (sodium hydroxide) and phenolphthalein can irritate eyes and skin
  • Always wear gloves when handling solutions
  • Alert instructor immediately if any spills occur
  • Wash hands thoroughly after the exercise

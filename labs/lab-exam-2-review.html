Methyl Red (MR) & Voges-Proskauer (VP) Tests

MRVP Broth

Purpose

Both tests use the same MRVP broth to differentiate bacteria based on how they ferment glucose - specifically what end products they produce.

Methyl Red Test
  • Detects stable acid end products from glucose fermentation
  • Add 15 drops Methyl Red to MRVP broth
  • Mix well - instant color change
  • Methyl Red is a pH indicator
Voges-Proskauer Test
  • Detects acetoin + butanediol production
  • Transfer 1 mL of broth
  • Add 9 drops VPA, mix
  • Add 3 drops VPB, mix
  • Wait 15-20 min (read every 10 min for 45 min)
  • Do NOT shake tubes after reagents added

Results Interpretation

MR Positive (+)

RED color = Acid produced (low pH)

E. coli (+)
MR Negative (-)

No color change = No stable acids

Enterobacter (-) Pseudomonas (-)
VP Positive (+)

RED color at top = Acetoin produced

Don't confuse with copper color!

Enterobacter (+)
VP Negative (-)

No red color = No acetoin

E. coli (-)
Key Concept: MR(+) organisms and VP(+) organisms are typically mutually exclusive! If an organism is MR(+), it's usually VP(-), and vice versa. This is because they represent different fermentation pathways - mixed acid fermentation (MR+) vs. butanediol fermentation (VP+).
Quick Study
  • MR+ = RED (acid), VP+ = RED at top (acetoin)
  • MR reagent: Methyl red
  • VP reagents: Barritt's A (alpha-naphthol), B (KOH)
  • MR and VP are usually opposite (mutually exclusive)

Phenol Red Broth (Durham Tubes)

Carbohydrate Fermentation

Purpose

Tests the ability of bacteria to ferment specific sugars (glucose, lactose, sucrose, etc.) and whether gas is produced during fermentation.

Medium Components
  • Simple broth with a specific sugar
  • Sugar can vary (glucose, lactose, sucrose, etc.)
  • Phenol Red pH indicator
  • Durham tube (small inverted tube) to trap gas
Phenol Red pH Ranges
  • pH < 6.8 = Yellow (acidic)
  • pH 6.9-7.3 = Red (neutral)
  • pH > 7.4 = Pink/Magenta (alkaline)

Results Interpretation

Color Gas Bubble Result (Acid/Gas) Interpretation
Yellow Yes +/+ Ferments sugar, produces acid AND gas
Yellow No +/- Ferments sugar, produces acid only
Red No -/- Does NOT ferment sugar (control)
Pink/Magenta No -/- (protein) Protein degradation (alkaline products)
Key Concept: Gas will ONLY be produced if there is also acid! You cannot have a -/+ result (no acid but gas). The gas (usually CO2 and H2) is a byproduct of fermentation that only occurs when sugars are being broken down.
Quick Study
  • Yellow = Acid (+), Red = No fermentation (-)
  • Bubble in Durham tube = Gas (+)
  • Results written as Acid/Gas: +/+, +/-, -/-
  • Cannot have -/+ (no gas without acid!)
  • Pink/Magenta = protein breakdown (alkaline)

Indole Test

Tryptophan Metabolism

Purpose

Determines if bacteria can produce the enzyme tryptophanase to break down the amino acid tryptophan into indole, pyruvate, and ammonia.

Biochemistry
  • Tryptophan + H2O + tryptophanase
  • Produces: Indole + Pyruvate + NH3
  • Indole reacts with Kovac's reagent
  • Forms rosindole dye (cherry red)
Procedure
  • Grow bacteria in tryptone broth
  • Add 15 drops Kovac's Reagent
  • Look for color change at surface
  • Results are immediate

Results Interpretation

Indole Positive (+)

RED ring at top of broth

Tryptophanase enzyme present

E. coli (+)
Indole Negative (-)

No color change (reagent stays yellow)

No tryptophanase enzyme

Enterobacter (-) Klebsiella (-)
Key Concept: The Indole test is part of the IMViC tests (Indole, Methyl Red, Voges-Proskauer, Citrate) used to differentiate Enterobacteriaceae. E. coli is I+ M+ V- C- while Enterobacter is I- M- V+ C+.
Quick Study
  • Indole+ = RED ring at top after adding Kovac's reagent
  • Tests for tryptophanase enzyme (breaks down tryptophan)
  • Part of IMViC tests: Indole, MR, VP, Citrate

Kligler Iron Agar (KIA) / TSI

Multiple Test Medium

Purpose

A multipurpose medium that tests for glucose fermentation, lactose/sucrose fermentation, gas production, and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) production all in one tube!

Medium Components
  • Glucose: 1g/L (0.1%)
  • Lactose & Sucrose: 10g each/L (1%)
  • Phenol Red: pH indicator
  • Sodium thiosulfate: Sulfur source
  • Ferrous sulfate: H2S indicator
Reading the Tube
  • Slant (top): Aerobic conditions
  • Butt (bottom): Anaerobic conditions
  • Report as: Slant/Butt, H2S, Gas
  • Example: K/A, H2S(-), Gas(+)

The Reversion Concept

Why the slant can change color:
  1. Glucose is preferred but only 0.1% (runs out in ~12 hours)
  2. 0.1% glucose fermentation produces small amount of acid (yellow)
  3. When glucose depleted, bacteria use beef extract/peptone
  4. Protein degradation produces NH3 (ammonia) - raises pH
  5. Slant REVERTS to red/pink (alkaline), but butt stays yellow

Results Interpretation

Slant/Butt Abbreviation Interpretation
Red/Yellow K/A (Alkaline/Acid) Glucose fermenter ONLY (reversion occurred)
Yellow/Yellow A/A (Acid/Acid) Glucose AND lactose/sucrose fermenter
Red/Red K/K Non-fermenter (uses peptones)
Black precipitate H2S (+) Hydrogen sulfide production (FeS)

H2S Production

How H2S is Detected
  • Sugar fermentation produces acids (H+)
  • Cysteine desulfurase only works in acidic environment
  • Breaks down cysteine to pyruvate + H2S gas
  • H2S + Fe (ferrous sulfate) = FeS (black precipitate)
  • Any black color = H2S positive

KIA Notation Legend

Format: Slant/Butt/Hâ‚‚S/Gas

  • K = Alkaline (red/pink) - no fermentation or reversion
  • A = Acid (yellow) - fermentation occurred
  • + = Positive (Hâ‚‚S = black, Gas = bubbles/cracks)
  • - = Negative

  1. K/K/-/- â€” No fermentation, no Hâ‚‚S, no gas
  2. K/A/+/- â€” Glucose only, Hâ‚‚S positive, no gas
  3. K/A/+/+ â€” Glucose only, Hâ‚‚S positive, gas positive
  4. K/A/-/- â€” Glucose only, no Hâ‚‚S, no gas
  5. K/A/-/+ â€” Glucose only, no Hâ‚‚S, gas positive

Quick Study
  • Results: Slant/Butt/Hâ‚‚S/Gas (K=alkaline, A=acid)
  • Slant (top) = Lactose; Butt (bottom) = Glucose
  • Black color = Hâ‚‚S positive (iron reacts with sulfide)

EMB Agar (Eosin Methylene Blue)

Selective & Differential

Purpose

A selective AND differential medium used to detect coliform bacteria (indicators of fecal contamination) and differentiate lactose fermenters.

Selective Properties
  • Encourages growth of Gram (-) organisms
  • Inhibits growth of Gram (+) organisms
  • Dyes (eosin & methylene blue) inhibit Gram (+)
Medium Components
  • Lactose: Fermentable sugar
  • Eosin Y: pH indicator dye
  • Methylene Blue: pH indicator dye
  • Both dyes also inhibit Gram (+)

Results Interpretation

Appearance Interpretation Example Organisms
Green metallic sheen VIGOROUS lactose fermenter (strong acid) E. coli - indicates fecal contamination!
Pink/Purple colonies Lactose fermenter (weak) Enterobacter, Klebsiella
Colorless/Color of medium Lactose NON-fermenter Salmonella, Shigella, Pseudomonas
No growth Gram (+) organism (inhibited) Staphylococcus, Streptococcus
Key Concept: The green metallic sheen is the KEY indicator of fecal contamination! It's produced by E. coli's vigorous fermentation of lactose, which causes the dyes to precipitate on the colony surface in a characteristic iridescent pattern.
Quick Study
  • Green metallic sheen = strong lactose fermenter
  • Purple/Pink = weak lactose fermenter
  • Colorless = Lactose NON-fermenter
  • Selective: Inhibits Gram (+) bacteria
  • Differential: Shows lactose fermentation ability

Catalase Test

Toxic Oxygen Removal

Purpose

Tests for the presence of catalase enzyme, which breaks down toxic hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) into water and oxygen gas.

Why Catalase Matters
  • The ETC produces toxic H2O2 as byproduct
  • H2O2 is damaging to cells
  • Catalase detoxifies: 2H2O2 â†’ 2H2O + O2
  • Bacteria need catalase to do aerobic respiration
Procedure
  • Use a glass slide
  • Use applicator stick to smear cells
  • Add 1-2 drops of H2O2 (3%)
  • Look for bubbling (O2 release)

Results Interpretation

Catalase Positive (+)

Bubbling/Fizzing

Oxygen gas being released

Staphylococcus (+)
Catalase Negative (-)

No bubbles

No catalase enzyme

Streptococcus (-)
Clinical Application: The catalase test is the quickest way to differentiate Staphylococcus (catalase +, clusters) from Streptococcus (catalase -, chains). Both are Gram-positive cocci!
Quick Study
  • Bubbles = Positive (Oâ‚‚ gas released)
  • No bubbles = Negative
  • Key test to differentiate Staph (+) from Strep (-)

Mannitol Salt Agar (MSA)

Selective & Differential

Purpose

A selective AND differential medium used to isolate Staphylococcus species and differentiate pathogenic from non-pathogenic staphylococci.

Medium Components
  • Beef extract & Peptone: Nutrients
  • NaCl (7.5%): High salt = SELECTIVE
  • Mannitol: Fermentable sugar = DIFFERENTIAL
  • Phenol Red: pH indicator
Why 7.5% Salt?
  • Normal bacterial cell = 0.85% salt
  • 7.5% salt = nearly 10x higher!
  • Most bacteria can't survive
  • Staphylococcus is HALOTOLERANT

Results Interpretation

Growth? Color Change? Interpretation
Yes Yellow (acidic) Staphylococcus that ferments mannitol
= Likely PATHOGENIC (S. aureus)
Yes No change (red) Staphylococcus that does NOT ferment mannitol
= Likely NON-pathogenic (S. epidermidis)
No N/A Not halotolerant (not Staphylococcus)
Key Concept: NaCl is SELECTIVE (selects for salt-tolerant Staph), while Mannitol + Phenol Red is DIFFERENTIAL (differentiates mannitol fermenters from non-fermenters). Yellow zones indicate pathogenic S. aureus!
Quick Study
  • Growth + Yellow = Mannitol fermenter (pathogenic Staph)
  • Growth + Red = Non-fermenter
  • No growth = Not Staphylococcus
  • Selective: High salt selects for Staph
  • Differential: Yellow = mannitol fermentation

Nitrate Reduction Test

Anaerobic Respiration

Purpose

Tests whether bacteria can perform anaerobic respiration using nitrate (NO3-) as the terminal electron acceptor instead of oxygen.

Denitrification Pathway

NO3- â†’ NO2- â†’ NO â†’ N2O â†’ N2

  • Nitrate â†’ Nitrite â†’ Nitric oxide
  • â†’ Nitrous oxide â†’ Nitrogen gas
  • Occurs when O2 is not available
Procedure
  • Grow in nitrate broth
  • Add 6 drops Nitrate Reagent A
  • Add 6 drops Nitrate Reagent B
  • Wait 2 minutes, check for red
  • If no red, add zinc powder

Results Interpretation (This is tricky!)

After Reagents A&B After Zinc Interpretation
RED N/A (don't add) Positive: NO3 reduced to NO2 (nitrite detected)
No color RED Negative: Zinc reduced remaining NO3 to NO2
No color No color Positive: Complete denitrification (NO3 â†’ N2)
Why the confusion?
  • Reagents detect NITRITE (NO2-), not nitrate
  • If bacteria reduced NO3 to NO2, reagents turn RED (positive)
  • If no red, maybe NO3 is still there (unreduced) OR completely reduced to N2
  • Zinc chemically reduces NO3 to NO2 - if it turns red, NO3 was still there = NEGATIVE
  • If zinc doesn't cause red, NO3 was completely reduced to N2 = POSITIVE
Quick Study
  • RED after reagents = Positive (nitrite present)
  • Reagents: Nitrate A (sulfanilic acid), B (dimethyl-alpha-naphthylamine)
  • No red? Add zinc powder:
  • Red after zinc = Negative; No red after zinc = Positive

Motility Test (SIM Medium)

Bacterial Movement

Purpose

Tests whether bacteria are motile (capable of self-propelled movement) using flagella. SIM medium can also test for Indole production and Hâ‚‚S production simultaneously.

SIM Medium Components
  • S = Sulfide: Ferrous sulfate + sodium thiosulfate for Hâ‚‚S detection
  • I = Indole: Tryptophan for indole production
  • M = Motility: Semi-solid agar (0.5%) allows movement
  • Low agar concentration creates semi-solid consistency
Inoculation Technique
  • Use inoculating needle (NOT loop)
  • Stab straight down into center of medium
  • Remove needle along same path
  • Incubate and observe for spreading growth

Results Interpretation

Motile (+)

Diffuse, cloudy growth spreading away from stab line

Growth throughout the medium

E. coli (+) Proteus (+) Pseudomonas (+)
Non-Motile (-)

Growth only along stab line

Sharp, defined line of growth

Klebsiella (-) Shigella (-) Staphylococcus (-)

Types of Bacterial Flagella

  • Monotrichous: Single flagellum at one end (e.g., Vibrio)
  • Amphitrichous: Single flagellum at both ends
  • Lophotrichous: Tuft of flagella at one or both ends (e.g., Pseudomonas)
  • Peritrichous: Flagella all around the cell (e.g., E. coli, Salmonella)
Key Concepts:
  • Semi-solid agar (0.5%) allows motile bacteria to swim away from stab
  • Motile = diffuse/cloudy growth spreading from stab line
  • Non-motile = sharp growth only along stab line
  • Shigella is non-motile (key differentiator from Salmonella)
  • Flagella provide chemotaxis - movement toward nutrients/away from toxins
  • TTC (2,3,5-Triphenyltetrazolium chloride) may be added - turns red when reduced by bacteria, making growth more visible
Quick Study
  • Diffuse/cloudy growth = Motile (+)
  • Growth only along stab line = Non-motile (-)
  • SIM medium: Sulfide, Indole, Motility
  • Use inoculating needle (not loop) - stab technique

Exercise 13: Capsular Staining (Anthony Method)

Lab Exercise

What is a Capsule?

  • Capsule: Distinct, gelatinous layer outside the cell wall
  • Slime layer: Diffuse, irregular layer (less organized)
  • Glycocalyx: Term for polysaccharide capsules ("sugar shell")
  • Exception: Bacillus anthracis has a protein capsule, not polysaccharide

Functions of Capsules (Virulence Factors)

  • Anti-phagocytic - Prevents engulfment by WBCs (S. pneumoniae)
  • Attachment - Helps adhere to surfaces (S. mutans - dental caries)
  • Biofilm formation - Dental plaque

Anthony Method - Critical Points

DO NOT HEAT-FIX! Heat destroys or shrinks the capsule.
  • Air-dry only - No heat fixation
  • Primary stain: Crystal violet (2 min)
  • Wash with: 20% copper sulfate (NOT water)
  • Copper sulfate acts as both decolorizer and counterstain
Background

Purple (crystal violet + milk proteins)

Cell Body

Dark purple

Capsule

Clear or light blue halo

Test-Taking Points:
  • Capsules = major virulence factor
  • S. pneumoniae capsule prevents phagocytosis
  • S. mutans capsule causes dental plaque/caries
  • Anthony method: NO heat-fix, use copper sulfate wash
  • Capsule appears as clear halo around dark purple cells
  • Example organism: Klebsiella pneumoniae (has prominent capsule)
Quick Study
  • Clear halo around purple cells = Capsule
  • Stain: Crystal violet; Wash: Copper sulfate
  • Anthony method: NO heat-fix
  • Capsule = virulence factor (prevents phagocytosis)

  • Only produced by Bacillus and Clostridium genera

GREEN (malachite green retained)

Exercise 16: Acid-Fast Staining (Kinyoun Method)

Lab Exercise

What Makes Bacteria Acid-Fast?

  • Mycolic acid - waxy lipid in cell wall (up to 80 carbons long)
  • Makes cell wall nearly impermeable to water-soluble dyes
  • Once stained, dye is NOT removed by acid-alcohol
  • "Acid-fast" = resists decolorization by acid-alcohol

Acid-Fast Organisms

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis â†’ Tuberculosis (TB)
  • Mycobacterium leprae â†’ Leprosy (Hansen's disease)
  • Some Nocardia species â†’ Various infections

Two Acid-Fast Staining Methods

  • Ziehl-Neelsen (hot): Requires heat - phenol fumes hazardous
  • Kinyoun (cold): No heat needed - safer, no toxic fumes

Kinyoun Method Steps

  1. Primary stain: Basic fuchsin (5 min) - stains all cells RED
  2. Decolorizer: Acid-alcohol (~1 min) - removes stain from non-acid-fast cells
  3. Counterstain: Methylene blue (30 sec) - stains decolorized cells BLUE
Acid-Fast Positive

RED/PINK (M. smegmatis - rods)

Non-Acid-Fast

BLUE (S. epidermidis - cocci)

Test-Taking Points:
  • Acid-fast = RED (retains primary stain)
  • Non-acid-fast = BLUE (takes counterstain)
  • Mycolic acid is responsible for the acid-fast property
  • Critical for diagnosing TB and leprosy
  • Kinyoun method is safer than Ziehl-Neelsen (no heating)
  • Only Mycobacterium and some Nocardia are acid-fast
  • Regular Gram stain does NOT work reliably on mycobacteria
Quick Study
  • RED = Acid-fast positive
  • BLUE = Non-acid-fast
  • Stain: Carbol fuchsin; Counterstain: Methylene blue
  • Decolorizer: Acid-alcohol
  • Mycolic acid in cell wall = acid-fast property

Exercise 56: A Synthetic Epidemic

Lab Exercise

Essential Epidemiology Terms

  • Endemic: Steady frequency of disease in a region over time
  • Epidemic: Excessive cases in an area compared to normal
  • Pandemic: Epidemic spread across continents (e.g., COVID-19)
  • Index case: First identified case in an outbreak
  • Carrier: Person who harbors and transmits pathogen but shows no symptoms
  • Fomite: Inanimate object that transmits pathogens (doorknob, phone)
  • Vector: Living carrier (insect/rodent) that transmits pathogen

Two Types of Epidemics

  • Common Source: Single contaminated source (food, water); rapid onset; sharp peak, rapid decline; Example: E. coli from lettuce
  • Host-to-Host (Propagated): Infected individuals; gradual onset; gradual rise, slower decline; Example: Influenza, COVID-19

Herd Immunity

When a sufficient portion of a population is immune, outbreak is unlikely because spread is limited.
  • Protects those who cannot be vaccinated (infants, immunocompromised)
  • Higher R0 (reproduction number) = higher threshold needed
  • Measles: 92-95% threshold
  • Polio: 80-86% threshold
  • COVID-19 (original): 60-70% threshold
  • Influenza: 33-50% threshold

Lab Simulation Results

  • 100% susceptible â†’ Many become infected
  • 50% vaccinated â†’ Fewer infections, spread reduced
  • 90% vaccinated â†’ Very few or no infections (herd immunity achieved)

In lab: NaOH = "infected", pH buffer = "vaccinated", water = "susceptible"

Pink color with phenolphthalein = positive (infected)

Quick Study
  • Endemic = steady; Epidemic = excess; Pandemic = global
  • Common source = sharp peak; Propagated = gradual rise
  • R0 = reproduction number (higher = more contagious)
  • Fomite = object; Vector = living carrier
  • Index case = first identified case in outbreak
Test-Taking Points:
  • Endemic = constant presence; Epidemic = excessive cases; Pandemic = global spread
  • Index case = first identified case - epidemiologists trace backward from this
  • Common source epidemic = sharp peak (contaminated food), propagated = gradual rise (person-to-person)
  • Herd immunity protects unvaccinated individuals when enough population is immune
  • R0 = basic reproduction number (average # infected by one person)
  • Fomite = inanimate object; Vector = living carrier
  • Higher R0 = higher herd immunity threshold needed

Exercise 60: ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay)

Lab Exercise

Key Terminology

  • Antigen: Molecule that triggers immune response; recognized by antibodies
  • Antibody: Protein (immunoglobulin) produced by B cells that binds antigens
  • Immunoassay: Test using labeled antibodies to detect antigen-antibody interactions
  • Enzyme-linked: Antibody has enzyme attached that produces color when substrate added

The Key Question: What Are We Looking For?

  • Direct ELISA: Looking for unknown ANTIGENS (pathogen itself)
  • Indirect ELISA: Looking for ANTIBODIES (immune response to pathogen)

Remember: "Direct = Detect pathogen, Indirect = Detect immune response"

Direct (Sandwich) ELISA - Detecting Antigens

Purpose: Find unknown antigens (pathogens) in a sample

Example: COVID rapid test, detecting bacteria in throat swab

  1. Coat wells with known "capture" antibody - This antibody will grab the target antigen
  2. Add patient sample - If antigen present, it binds to capture antibody
  3. Wash - Remove unbound material
  4. Add enzyme-linked "detection" antibody - Binds other side of antigen (makes "sandwich")
  5. Wash again
  6. Add substrate - Enzyme produces COLOR if antigen was present
The Sandwich: Capture Antibody â†’ Antigen â†’ Detection Antibody (enzyme-linked)

Indirect ELISA - Detecting Antibodies (HIV Test in Lab)

Purpose: Find antibodies in patient serum (shows past/current infection)

Example: HIV antibody test, hepatitis screening

  1. Coat wells with known antigen (e.g., HIV proteins)
  2. Add patient serum - If patient has anti-HIV antibodies, they bind to antigen
  3. Wash - Remove unbound material
  4. Add secondary antibody - Anti-human IgG with enzyme attached (binds to patient's antibodies)
  5. Wash again
  6. Add substrate - Enzyme produces COLOR if patient antibodies were present
The Chain: Known Antigen â†’ Patient's Antibody â†’ Secondary Antibody (enzyme-linked)
Positive (+)

GREEN color develops

Antigen (direct) or antibodies (indirect) DETECTED

Negative (-)

COLORLESS - no color change

No antigen or antibodies detected

Lab Controls

  • PBS (negative control): Expected colorless - confirms no false positives
  • Positive control: Expected green - confirms test is working
Positive ELISA requires confirmatory testing (Western blot or nucleic acid test)
Quick Study
  • Direct ELISA = detects ANTIGENS (pathogen)
  • Indirect ELISA = detects ANTIBODIES (immune response)
  • GREEN = positive; Colorless = negative
  • Enzyme on secondary antibody converts substrate to color
  • ELISA = screening test (needs confirmation)
Test-Taking Points:
  • ELISA = Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay
  • Direct/Sandwich ELISA = detects ANTIGENS (the pathogen)
  • Indirect ELISA = detects ANTIBODIES (patient's immune response)
  • GREEN = positive; Colorless = negative
  • Enzyme (peroxidase) converts substrate to colored product
  • Washing steps are critical to remove unbound material
  • ELISA is a screening test - positive results need confirmation
  • Uses: HIV, hepatitis, West Nile virus, syphilis, COVID-19

Exercise 61: Blood Grouping (ABO & Rh Typing)

Lab Exercise

ABO Blood Types - Antigens & Antibodies

  • Type A: A antigens on RBCs, Anti-B antibodies in serum
  • Type B: B antigens on RBCs, Anti-A antibodies in serum
  • Type AB: Both A and B antigens, Neither antibody (universal recipient)
  • Type O: Neither A nor B antigens, Both anti-A and anti-B (universal donor)

Blood Typing Results - Agglutination Patterns

  • A+: Anti-A (+), Anti-B (-), Anti-Rh (+)
  • B+: Anti-A (-), Anti-B (+), Anti-Rh (+)
  • AB+: Anti-A (+), Anti-B (+), Anti-Rh (+)
  • O+: Anti-A (-), Anti-B (-), Anti-Rh (+)
  • O-: Anti-A (-), Anti-B (-), Anti-Rh (-)

Agglutination = clumping (grainy); No agglutination = smooth, milky

Universal Donor & Recipient

  • O- (Universal Donor): Can donate to all types, can only receive from O-
  • AB+ (Universal Recipient): Can only donate to AB+, can receive from all types

Rh Factor and Hemolytic Disease of the Newborn (HDN)

Problem: Rh- mother + Rh+ fetus (inherited from father)
  • First pregnancy: Mother exposed to Rh+ fetal cells during birth
  • Sensitization: Mother produces anti-D antibodies (IgG)
  • Second pregnancy: IgG crosses placenta, attacks Rh+ fetal RBCs
  • Result: Hemolysis â†’ jaundice, anemia, potentially fatal
Prevention: RhoGAM (Rh immune globulin) given at week 28 and after delivery

Blood Type Frequencies (US)

  • O+: ~38% (most common)
  • A+: ~34%
  • B+: ~9%
  • O-: ~7%
  • AB+: ~3%
  • AB-: ~1% (rarest)
Quick Study
  • Type O- = universal donor; Type AB+ = universal recipient
  • Agglutination = clumping = positive test result
  • HDN: Rh- mother + Rh+ baby (2nd pregnancy risk)
  • RhoGAM prevents mother from making anti-D antibodies
  • Antibodies in serum are opposite of antigens on RBCs
Test-Taking Points:
  • Type O = universal donor (no A or B antigens to react)
  • Type AB = universal recipient (no anti-A or anti-B antibodies)
  • Agglutination = clumping = positive reaction with antisera
  • Rh+ = has D antigen; Rh- = lacks D antigen
  • HDN occurs when Rh- mother has Rh+ baby (second pregnancy)
  • RhoGAM prevents mother from making anti-D antibodies
  • Developed by Karl Landsteiner (~1900)
  • Type O most common (45%); Type AB rarest (4%)

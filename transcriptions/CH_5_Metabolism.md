# Chapter 5: Metabolism

## Slide 1: Redox Reactions
- Another way of transferring energy
- Transfer electrons
- Reduction
  - Gain electrons
- Oxidation
  - Loss of electrons
- LEO says GER
  - Loss of Electrons = Oxidation
  - Gain of Electrons = Reduction

- Linked reactions
  - A → B → C → D
- Product of one reaction becomes reactant of another
- Proceed in a systematic highly regulated manner
- Each reaction requires a unique, specific enzyme
- Enzymes increase rates of biological reactions

## Slide 6: Aerobic Respiration
- Process requires oxygen, gives off carbon dioxide
- Usually involves breakdown of glucose to CO₂ and H₂O
- Pretty much the reverse of photosynthesis

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (ATP)

**Figure: Mitochondria diagram showing glucose and oxygen entering, producing CO₂, H₂O, and ATP**

## Slide 7: Tricarboxylic Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle)
- Occurs in cytoplasm of prokaryotes
  - Eukaryotes: mitochondrial matrix
- Series of chemical reactions finish breakdown of glucose
- Pyruvic acid converted to acetyl CoA
- NAD⁺ reduced to NADH
- NADH and FADH₂ are electron carriers
  - Pass high energy electrons to ETC

## Slide 8: Metabolic Reactions
- Metabolism: sum of all cellular chemical reactions
- Anabolic reaction
  - Biosynthesis
  - Forms larger macromolecules from smaller
  - Requires energy
  - Endergonic
- Catabolic reaction
  - Degradative
  - Breaks bonds of larger molecules forming smaller
  - Releases energy
  - Exergonic

## Slide 20: Fermentation
- Pyruvic acid from glycolysis
- Converted to organic product
- Does not require oxygen
- Does not use TCA or ETC
- Generates small amounts of ATP
- Different organisms generate different end products
  - Lactic acid
  - Alcohol.

**Figure: (a) Flowchart showing Glycolysis (Glucose → 2 Pyruvic acid, producing 2 ATP and 2 NADH) → Formation of fermentation end-products. ## Slide 21: ATP
- Adenosine triphosphate
- The energy currency of the cell
- High energy compound used to drive metabolic reactions
- Chemical PE in ATP is the principal source of usable energy in cells.

**Figure: Diagram showing ATP structure with Adenine + Ribose + three Phosphate groups (P-P-P). Below shows hydrolysis reaction: ATP + H₂O → Adenosine Diphosphate (ADP) + P + Energy**

## Slide 22: Chemiosmosis
- Diffusion of H⁺
  - Back to cytoplasm
- Energy derived from the flow of H⁺ is used to synthesize ATP
  - (from ADP and Pi)
- ATP Synthase.

**Figure: Photo of Hoover Dam illustrating flow/gradient concept. Diagrams showing (1) metabolic pathway overview and (2) detailed membrane diagram with electron transport chain pumping H⁺ to create high H⁺ concentration, then H⁺ flowing through ATP synthase to produce ATP from ADP + P**

## Slide 23: Control of Enzyme Activity
- Competitive inhibition
  - Mimics substrate
  - Occupies enzyme active site
  - Prevents real substrate from binding
  - Lots of drugs and antimicrobials work this way
    - Sulfonilamide (sulfa) inhibits PABA binding to its enzyme.

**Figure: Diagram showing Competitive Inhibition - Normal substrate and competitive inhibitor (with similar shape) both compete for enzyme active site. ## Slide 25: Factors Influencing Enzymatic Activity
- Temperature
- Rate of chemical reactions increase
  - With a temperature increase
    - Up to a point
  - Protein denaturation

    - Loss of catalytic activity.

**Figure: Graph showing Enzymatic activity vs Temperature (°C), scale 10-50°C. Curve rises then falls sharply after ~40°C. Caption: "(a) Temperature. ## Slide 26: Enzymes and Activation Energy
- Enzymes can only speed reactions that are possible
- Energy of Activation (E_A)
  - The energy needed to make a reaction proceed
  - Energy needed to rearrange electrons
- Enzymes lower the activation energy
- Enzymes are catalysts
  - They increase rate of reaction
  - Not a reactant or product
  - Not consumed in reaction
  - They don't get permanently altered.

**Figure: Energy diagram showing Reactant (A B) at Initial energy level, two pathways (Reaction without enzyme - higher peak, Reaction with enzyme - lower peak), leading to Products (A B) at Final energy level. Labels show Activation energy without enzyme and Activation energy with enzyme**

## Slide 27: Electron Transport Chain and Chemiosmosis
- Pass electrons along
  - NADH oxidized to NAD⁺
- Energy released through electron transport used to create H⁺ gradient (potential energy)
  - Proton motive force
  - Necessary to make ATP
- Oxygen (O₂) is essential for electron transport!
  - It is the final electron acceptor in the ETC
  - Accepts 2 electrons and gets reduced to water.

## Slide 31: Lactic Acid Fermentation
- Pyruvic acid from glycolysis
- Reduced by NADH
- NAD⁺ regenerated used to continue glycolysis
- Results in lactic acid
- Low ATP yield.

**Figure: Diagram showing Glycolysis pathway: Glucose → 2 Pyruvic acid (producing 2 ATP, with 2 NAD⁺ → 2 NADH). Then branches to either:
(a) Lactic acid fermentation: Pyruvic acid (COOH-C=O-CH₃) → (2 NADH + 2H⁺ → 2 NAD⁺) → 2 Lactic acid (COOH-CHOH-CH₃)
(b) Alcohol fermentation: Pyruvic acid → (releases 2 CO₂) → 2 Acetaldehyde (CHO-CH₃) → (2 NADH + 2H⁺ → 2 NAD⁺) → 2 Ethanol (CH₂OH-CH₃)**

- When oxygen is not the final electron acceptor
  - Denitrification
    - Nitrate NO₃⁻
    - Nitrite NO₂⁻

- Not as much ATP produced as aerobic respiration.
